# Sahul Glacial Maximum Geography and Climate (~2 MYA)

Status: Draft

## Summary

At approximately 2 million years ago, Sahul (the combined landmass of Australia, New Guinea, and Tasmania during low sea levels) experienced repeated glacial maximum periods characterized by lower global temperatures, reduced atmospheric CO₂, and substantially lowered sea levels.

These conditions exposed continental shelves, connected previously isolated landmasses, altered rainfall patterns, expanded interior aridity, and reshaped the distribution of rainforest, woodland, and orchard-capable ecologies.

This environmental state defines the geographic and climatic foundation upon which early cassowary agricultural, preservation, and settlement systems emerge.

## Definition

This document describes the geography, coastline configuration, climate patterns, and ecological distributions of Sahul during a representative glacial maximum interval around ~2 MYA.

It focuses on:

- exposed continental shelves
- land bridges and connectivity
- rainfall and aridity gradients
- rainforest and orchard viability zones
- major ecological corridors and barriers

These conditions directly constrain food production, transport networks, and civilizational expansion.

## Mechanism / Process

Glacial maximum geography emerges from global climatic cooling and ice sheet expansion.

Primary drivers:

- large volumes of water locked in polar ice sheets
- global sea level drop of approximately 60–120 meters
- reduced evaporation and weaker hydrological cycles
- lower atmospheric CO₂ reducing plant productivity in marginal zones

These mechanisms reshape Sahul's physical and ecological structure.

### 1. Continental shelf exposure and landmass expansion

Lower sea levels expose shallow continental shelves.

Major exposed regions include:

- Arafura Shelf connecting northern Australia and southern New Guinea
- Torres Strait land bridge
- Gulf of Carpentaria transforming into an inland basin or seasonal lake system
- Sahul Shelf extending northern and western coastlines outward hundreds of kilometers in some regions

Result:

Australia and New Guinea form a continuous continent.

Movement between them becomes possible entirely on land.

This removes a major barrier to species and cultural exchange.

### 2. New Guinea highlands as stable wet refugia

New Guinea's central mountain spine reaches elevations over 4,000 meters.

Effects:

- orographic rainfall maintains high precipitation even during global dry periods
- cloud forests and montane forests remain stable
- river headwaters remain perennial

These regions likely function as ecological refugia supporting:

- persistent rainforest ecosystems
- stable orchard species
- high biodiversity and productivity

This makes the highlands a likely early center of continuous agricultural experimentation.

### 3. Rainforest contraction and fragmentation in Australia

Lower rainfall reduces rainforest extent across northern and eastern Australia.

Rainforests retreat toward:

- coastal zones
- river corridors
- mountain ranges
- volcanic uplands

Large interior rainforest regions disappear or fragment.

Result:

orchard-suitable ecologies become patchy rather than continuous.

This creates isolated productivity nodes rather than continent-wide agricultural zones.

### 4. Expansion of dry woodland and grassland interiors

Interior Australia becomes substantially drier.

Major effects:

- expansion of savanna and semi-arid woodland
- formation or expansion of desert systems
- episodic inland lakes during wetter cycles, but long dry intervals

These regions remain traversable but less suitable for orchard agriculture.

They function more as:

transport corridors  
barriers  
seasonal grazing or hunting zones  

rather than agricultural cores.

### 5. Formation of new coastal plains and corridors

Exposed coastal shelves create broad flat lowlands.

These areas may support:

- river systems extending farther seaward
- fertile alluvial plains
- gradual ecological transitions from coast to interior

Some of these plains are temporary on geological timescales and will later flood when sea levels rise.

This creates future submerged archaeological and civilizational zones.

### 6. Temperature reduction and seasonal variability

Global temperatures during glacial maximum periods are cooler by several degrees Celsius.

Effects in Sahul include:

- reduced evaporation rates
- altered plant growing seasons
- slower decomposition and microbial activity in some regions

Cooler upland environments may improve preservation conditions for stored foods relative to tropical lowlands.

## Evolutionary or Historical Emergence

Glacial cycles began intensifying during the late Pliocene and early Pleistocene.

Approximate progression:

### Pre-glacial baseline (>3 MYA)

Higher sea levels isolate New Guinea and Australia.

Rainforests extend more broadly across northern Australia.

Species exchange between landmasses is limited.

### Early glacial cycles (3–2.5 MYA)

Sea levels fluctuate.

Temporary land bridges form intermittently.

Species and ecological exchange begin increasing.

### Major glacial maximum (~2 MYA representative state)

Sea levels fall substantially.

Australia, New Guinea, and Tasmania become connected.

Rainforests contract to refugia zones.

Interior aridity increases.

Ecological zones stabilize long enough to support long-term adaptation.

### Later glacial-interglacial cycling (after ~2 MYA)

Sea levels repeatedly rise and fall.

Land bridges periodically disappear and reappear.

Coastal settlements may be repeatedly isolated or submerged.

Civilizational expansion patterns are strongly influenced by these cycles.

## Timeline Placement

Representative glacial maximum state: ~2 MYA

Repeated similar states occur throughout early Pleistocene glacial cycles.

This environmental configuration overlaps with:

- honeypot ant domestication candidate timeline (~3–2 MYA)
- early preservation infrastructure emergence (provisional)
- early transport corridor formation (provisional)

## Constraints and Limits

### Ecological constraints

Rainforest-dependent agricultural systems cannot exist continuously across the continent.

They must cluster in ecological refugia.

Arid interior zones limit continuous orchard expansion.

### Geographic constraints

Land bridges exist only during low sea level periods.

Later sea level rise may isolate populations and sever transport routes.

### Preservation constraints

Hot lowland tropical environments accelerate spoilage.

Cool upland regions may be preferred storage and settlement locations.

### Transport constraints

Long-distance transport across dry interior zones may require seasonal timing or route specialization.

River corridors and upland chains likely form primary movement pathways.

## Civilizational Implications

This geography strongly shapes cassowary civilizational emergence.

### 1. New Guinea likely functions as a major early civilizational core

Stable rainfall and ecological continuity support:

- orchard domestication
- honeypot ant domestication
- long-term settlement continuity

### 2. Land bridges allow continental expansion

Cassowary populations and agricultural systems can spread into Australia without maritime technology.

This enables large-scale geographic expansion earlier than would otherwise be possible.

### 3. Agricultural regions emerge as ecological islands

Productive orchard regions exist as discrete nodes separated by less productive terrain.

This favors:

network-based civilization  
regional specialization  
transport corridors between productivity zones  

rather than uniform continental agriculture.

### 4. Coastal shelf settlements are vulnerable to future flooding

As sea levels rise during interglacial periods:

- coastal plains flood
- settlements may be abandoned
- populations retreat inland

This creates cycles of expansion and contraction.

### 5. Highland and volcanic regions become strategic centers

These regions combine:

- reliable rainfall
- fertile soils
- cooler storage conditions
- defensibility

They are likely early political and agricultural centers.
