## Summary

This technical document defines how preserved surplus becomes measurable, collectible, and enforceable in early cassowary institutions.

The core mechanism is a container-and-custody model: tribute is assessed in standardized sealed ceramic vessels (volume units), not abstract weights. Seal marks, witnessed counting, and controlled storage access allow obligations to be enforced before writing systems or formal accounting.

This document is `Draft` because key quantitative parameters (vessel volumes, spoilage/loss rates, and regional variation in custody institutions) remain unresolved.

## Definition

Tribute and taxation are recurring institutional systems for surplus extraction and redistribution in which:

- obligations are expressed in standardized physical units
- collected goods are transferred into recognized custody
- access to stored goods is regulated by social authority
- enforcement is achieved through control of storage, orchard access, and coalition power

In early cassowary civilizations, the primary taxable unit is expected to be a sealed volume container, especially for preserved sugar paste and related preserved foods.

## Mechanism or Model

This is a volume-measurement and custody-enforcement model.

Core logic:

- preservation creates durable surplus
- ceramic containers create repeatable volume units
- seals create tamper evidence and authority marks
- centralized custody creates leverage over redistribution
- repeated collection cycles create institutional obligation

Measurement emerges from container standardization before mass measurement or abstract mathematics. A settlement can demand "three sealed jars of the standard form" without needing scales or writing.

## Mechanism / Process

### 1. Tribute unit: standardized sealed vessel (volume before weight)

The earliest durable tribute unit is a standardized ceramic vessel with a fixed internal volume.

Expected properties of an early tribute vessel:

- consistent shape produced by repeated craft templates or practiced forms
- stable base and shoulder geometry for visual identification
- a narrow neck suitable for sealing
- an internal fill line or neck-threshold fill convention
- clay stamp zone or sealable mouth for authority marking
- breakable seal assembly that makes opening visible

Why volume is first:

- container volume is visually and materially repeatable
- cassowaries can compare vessel shapes without precision scales
- mass varies by moisture content and product type, but vessel count remains legible

Early taxation is therefore likely denominated as counts of sealed vessels, often content-specific (for example, preserved sugar paste jars counted separately from oil jars) rather than converted into abstract equivalent units.

### 2. Seal integrity and authority marking

A tribute vessel is not only filled; it is sealed into a custody state.

Plausible sealing sequence:

1. Vessel is filled to the accepted fill line or neck standard.
2. A plug or stopper (fiber, bark, ceramic lid, or similar closure) is inserted.
3. Resin, wax, clay, or layered sealant is applied across the closure.
4. One or more authority marks are impressed into the outer seal material.

Seal materials may vary by region and period, but the institutional function is constant: opening destroys or visibly disturbs the seal.

This enables trust without writing because:

- tampering becomes publicly inspectable
- custody transitions can be witnessed through new seal impressions
- a jar can display who last sealed it, even if no text exists

Early mark progression (provisional):

- household/lineage mark
- collection-node mark
- storehouse or elite/ritual authority mark

Multiple marks do not require literate recordkeeping; they function as recognized symbols pressed with claws, carved seal tools, or prepared stamp objects.

### 3. Custody and ownership model (who physically controls surplus)

Tribute institutions emerge by changing where sealed surplus is stored and who may authorize access.

#### Phase 1: Household storage (pre-tribute baseline)

- preserved goods remain in household or lineage custody
- obligations are social and situational, not standardized
- surplus can be hidden, consumed, or gifted without common measurement rules

Political leverage is low because control remains dispersed.

#### Phase 2: Communal storage grove / meeting storage

- households bring sealed containers to a shared storage zone during seasonal gatherings
- custody remains socially distributed but becomes publicly visible
- vessel counting begins to establish comparative contribution norms

Power begins to concentrate in those who host, guard, or witness the storage event.

#### Phase 3: Guarded storage house / protected cool store

- a designated storage structure or protected cool-storage site holds pooled sealed vessels
- entry and withdrawal are controlled by a recognized coalition
- custody shifts from "who produced it" toward "who controls access"

This is a major institutional threshold. Centralized custody allows rationing, delayed redistribution, and sanctions against noncompliant households.

#### Phase 4: Elite-, temple-, or court-controlled storage

- storage sites become authority centers, not just practical warehouses
- seal marks encode recognized political hierarchy
- tribute obligations become recurring and territory-linked

At this stage, ownership and custody diverge: producers may retain customary claims, but administrators or elites control when and how goods are released.

### 4. Collection mechanics (how tribute physically moves)

Tribute collection is a transport-and-custody process, not only an obligation rule.

Plausible early collection pattern:

- households or local groups fill and seal standard tribute vessels after preservation cycles
- vessels are hand-carried by individuals during seasonal gathering windows
- collection occurs at agreed nodes (orchard meeting grounds, storage groves, ridge camps, river junction settlements, or other recurring gathering sites)
- vessels are counted and sorted by visible type and seal status
- selected shares are retained locally and/or forwarded to regional storage nodes

This ties tribute directly to transport infrastructure. Where routes are poor or seasonal access fails, tribute collection becomes episodic and local rather than regional.

### 5. Intake, counting, and rotation without writing

Early administration can function before abstract accounting by combining visual counting, public witnessing, and physical arrangement.

Plausible intake practices:

- sorting vessels by type/size class
- counting by rows, clusters, or rack positions
- assigning storage areas by contributing lineage or locality
- applying a new intake seal to jars entering guarded custody
- rotating older sealed jars toward earlier redistribution or consumption

The key administrative record is initially spatial and social:

- where jars are placed
- how many are visibly present
- who witnessed their intake
- whose seals remain intact

Memory and public inspection are the primary audit system. Symbolic notation may emerge later, but is not required for early tribute enforcement.

### 6. Enforcement model (without police institutions)

Early tribute enforcement is achieved through control of survival-critical systems, not specialized police forces.

Primary enforcement mechanisms:

- control of access to centralized stored food during scarcity
- control of access to productive orchard zones or protected ant-management areas
- coalition dominance at storage sites and collection gatherings
- public sanction for seal tampering or contribution refusal
- priority redistribution to compliant households, allies, and dependents

In practical terms, control of storage equals control of emergency survival. This creates enforceable authority even when administrative institutions remain simple.

### 7. Administrative specialization emergence

Once tribute depends on standardized vessels, seals, custody transitions, and rotation, labor specialization becomes unavoidable.

Required recurring tasks:

- preparing and inspecting standard vessels
- producing and applying seal materials
- recognizing marks and verifying seal integrity
- guarding storage spaces and limiting unauthorized access
- sorting and rotating sealed stock
- coordinating collection gatherings and redistribution events

Early administrators likely emerge as part-time custodians attached to powerful lineages, ritual centers, or storage coalitions. Over time, reliable access to tribute stores supports more permanent custodial, supervisory, and craft-specialist roles.

This is the institutional bridge from surplus management to political class formation.

## Evolutionary or Historical Emergence

Tribute and taxation emerge gradually from preservation and storage practice. The sequence is expected to be uneven across Sahul, with some regions remaining household- or communal-scale much longer than others.

### Episodic contribution and gift storage (pre-institution phase)

Preserved surplus may be transferred to allies, kin groups, or dominant individuals without standardized units or recurring schedules.

- obligations are social, negotiable, and event-specific
- no stable tribute unit
- custody remains mostly local

### Proto-tribute via repeated vessel contributions (~3 MYA, provisional)

As preserved foods and ceramic storage become more common, communities begin recurring seasonal contributions using familiar vessel forms.

- containers are counted, but not fully standardized across regions
- seals may indicate household custody more than political authority
- enforcement depends on coalition pressure and reciprocal expectations

This phase establishes the habit of measurable contribution without a mature taxation apparatus.

### Standardized vessel tribute (~2.5 MYA, provisional)

Regional powers begin requiring tribute in recognized standard vessel classes, especially for preserved sugar paste and other high-value stored foods.

- vessel form and fill convention become politically meaningful
- seal marks distinguish collector and storehouse custody
- tribute obligations become more regular and territory-linked

This is the point where stored surplus becomes reliably convertible into institutional power.

### Regional storage-center taxation (~2 MYA, provisional)

Guarded storage houses or storage complexes operate as regional nodes receiving, holding, and redistributing tribute.

- tribute moves beyond local communal pooling into tiered custody
- administrators and guards become recurrent specialists
- political hierarchy stabilizes around control of storage, transport nodes, and redistribution timing

Adoption remains ecologically constrained and regionally uneven; not all cassowary societies need this degree of centralization.

## Timeline Placement

- Proto-tribute (repeated seasonal vessel contributions, weak standardization): ~3.2-2.8 MYA (provisional)
- Standardized sealed vessel tribute in some core regions: ~2.7-2.3 MYA (provisional; centered near ~2.5 MYA)
- Regional guarded storage centers and forwarded taxation networks: ~2.2-1.8 MYA (provisional; centered near ~2 MYA)

These placements are intended to remain consistent with honeypot-ant surplus dependence emerging as a major civilizational base by ~2.5-2 MYA.

## Constraints and Limits

Tribute systems are limited by measurement reliability, storage loss, transport capacity, and social enforcement costs.

### Measurement and container constraints

- vessel standardization is imperfect without mature ceramic control
- breakage and shape drift undermine trust in "equal" units
- fill-line disputes are likely where contents are viscous or mixed
- content heterogeneity limits cross-product equivalence without later accounting systems

### Seal and custody constraints

- sealants fail in heat, humidity, or repeated handling
- false marks or imitation seals may emerge once tribute gains value
- custody disputes increase when ownership and storage control diverge
- memory-based audit systems degrade at larger scale or over longer storage intervals

### Transport and collection constraints

- seasonal flooding, route damage, or conflict can interrupt collection cycles
- fragile ceramics impose carrying and packing limits
- collection distances can exceed the political value of recovered tribute
- dispersed populations may support only episodic tribute, not regular taxation

### Ecological and political constraints

- overextraction can damage orchard and ant-management systems
- concentrated storage increases catastrophic loss risk (fire, flood, contamination, seizure)
- coercive extraction without redistribution can collapse compliance coalitions
- regional ecological variation may prevent a single empire-wide vessel standard

## Civilizational Implications

Tribute and taxation are the institutional mechanism that converts preserved surplus into durable authority.

### Causal chain (core-logic framing)

preserved surplus -> standardized containers -> sealed measurable units -> centralized custody -> controlled redistribution -> recurring obligations -> administrative specialization -> stable hierarchy

### Major implications

- **State physics begins with containers:** Authority can demand counts of objects before it can demand abstract quantities.
- **Custody becomes power:** The group that controls sealed stores can reward allies and punish defectors without needing constant direct violence.
- **Ceramic and sealing craft gain political importance:** Container standardization and seal integrity become state-capacity multipliers.
- **Seasonal gatherings become administrative events:** Collection, seal inspection, and public counting create recurring moments of political visibility.
- **Ownership norms become more complex:** Producers, custodians, and rulers may each claim different rights over the same stored goods.
- **Administrative classes emerge gradually:** Jar counting, guarding, sealing, and rotation produce specialized roles before literate bureaucracy.
