## Key Atomic Notes

### Orchard Lineage Management

- Orchard lineage management preserves productive tree lineages instead of treating orchards as disposable annual plots.
- Cassowaries maintain functional orchard roles by selecting, propagating, replacing, and regionally substituting tree lineages over generations.
- Long maturation cycles make orchard planning slow, cumulative, and dependent on social continuity.
- Regional climate and soil conditions limit which lineages can be transferred without losing function.
- Lineage management lets the civilization preserve roles such as calorie base, buffer fruit, sap-flow host, nurse canopy, and boundary sacrifice tree even when species differ by region.
- Some orchard lineages are managed specifically as sap-flow host trees for honeydew production, not for fruit. Their value is measured in honeydew flow and aphid compatibility, not caloric yield from the tree itself.
- Fruit-and-sap dual trees are a distinct class: high-value trees that produce both cassowary-edible fruit and support sap-feeder populations on foliage and bark.
- Nurse canopy trees stabilise orchard microclimate and are especially important in wet-margin and upland systems, even though they are not direct sugar producers.
- Boundary and sacrifice trees absorb heavy sap-feeder pressure on orchard edges, protecting higher-value trees from overflow infestation.
- Grafted lineage trees represent accumulated technical investment. Knowing which traits to propagate and how is a form of expert knowledge with economic and political value.
- Orchard continuity is a prerequisite for stable sap-feeder management and downstream honeypot-ant systems.
- Manipulation capacity, tool use, ecological knowledge, and remembered social agreements all constrain how lineage management can work.

### Orchard Sugar Tree Lineages

- Tree choice controls honeydew flow. The choice of host tree is the upstream variable in the whole orchard-ant-tribute chain.
- Stable flow matters more than maximum flow. A tree that supports predictable moderate sap-feeder populations is more valuable than one that allows a spike and then declines.
- Aphid and sap-feeder overgrowth can damage and kill host trees. Managers must balance infestation density against tree health.
- Regional substitutions can preserve orchard function even when exact species differ. The functional role is what matters; the species name is secondary.
- Grafting and propagation preserve functional traits across generations when seed propagation does not reliably pass on those traits.
- Tree lineages become technical and possibly institutional knowledge. Knowing which trees to propagate, how, and where is expert knowledge with economic and political value.
- Long tree maturation cycles mean orchard investment is multigenerational. Trees planted in one generation may reach full productivity in the next.
- Tree loss — from disease, climate, over-infestation, or political disruption — is a long-cycle setback, not a short-cycle problem.
