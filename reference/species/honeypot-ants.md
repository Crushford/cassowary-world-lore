## Useful Baseline

- Honeypot ants are not a single species. They are ants from multiple unrelated lineages — including genera such as _Myrmecocystus_, _Camponotus_, _Melophorus_, and others — that independently evolved the use of specialised workers called repletes to store liquid food.
- Repletes can become greatly swollen with stored liquid carbohydrates, hanging from nest ceilings or clustering in protected chambers.
- Repletes are living storage bodies. They are not passive jars; they can move slowly, share stored liquid with nestmates, and respond to colony conditions.
- Repletes usually remain inside protected nest chambers rather than foraging outside.
- Honeypot ant storage behaviour is a real biological baseline, not a Cassowary World invention.
- Honeypot ant systems are especially associated with arid and semi-arid environments, where seasonal scarcity makes liquid food storage valuable. The strongest natural replete storage is found in dry or strongly seasonal landscapes.
- In wetter environments, colonies face increased risks: flooding, fungal growth in chambers, nest rot, and structural instability. These risks are real constraints, not minor inconveniences.
- In dry environments, colony survival is favoured, but sparse host plants and sap-feeder populations limit how much honeydew flows into the system. High per-colony storage in good years does not imply high landscape-scale output.
- Real-world honeypot ant colonies are not naturally equivalent to beehives and should not be assumed to scale easily. A managed beehive is not the right mental model.
- Colonies depend on local food flows, nest stability, humidity, temperature, and seasonal conditions. None of these are easy to transplant wholesale into a different ecology.
- Moving colonies long distances is difficult because they depend on specific nest structures, local food webs, and appropriate microhabitats. Any Cassowary World claim that colonies can be relocated requires a separate mechanism.
- Colony size, replete number, and total storage capacity vary with food availability, season, and colony age. Poorly productive seasons produce fewer and less-full repletes.

## Cassowary World Implications

Cassowary World systems build on the real replete-storage baseline, then add cassowary management, pottery-assisted nest architecture, regional ecotype selection, harvesting practices, and preservation. The baseline is dry-land arid; wet-zone and rainforest-margin systems require explicit managed expansion, not assumed equivalence.

Scalable colony propagation should reference the separate honeypot ant nest-splitting divergence. The reference file does not assume that property.
