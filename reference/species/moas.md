# Moas

Layer: Real-world reference
Status: Draft

## Summary

This file records baseline information about moas, the extinct flightless birds of New Zealand. It supports Cassowary World lore about New Zealand settlement, moa domestication, mounted transport, and later Sahul-New Zealand exchange.

## Metadata

- Primary topic: moas
- Topics: New Zealand, ratites, flightless birds, sexual size dimorphism, male parental care, Haast's eagle, extinction, domestication constraints
- Time periods: prehistoric New Zealand, with Cassowary World relevance before and around `~2.5-2 MYA`
- Regions: New Zealand
- Related species: cassowaries, dromornithids, Haast's eagle

## Atomic Notes

- Moas were extinct flightless birds native to New Zealand.
- Moas were ratites and should not be treated as close relatives of dromornithids or ducks.
- Moas had no functional wings, which makes any claim about manipulation, forelimb weapons, or arm-like traits a major Cassowary World divergence rather than baseline reference.
- Moa species varied widely in size, from relatively small forms to giant forms capable of standing several meters high with the neck extended.
- Some moa lineages showed strong sexual size dimorphism, with females substantially larger than males.
- Moas were herbivorous browsers and formed part of New Zealand forest, shrubland, and open-country ecosystems.
- Male parental care is plausible and useful for Cassowary World domestication logic, but exact breeding behavior should be handled cautiously unless supported by a dedicated reference pass.
- Haast's eagle was a major real-world moa predator and provides an important ecological reference for moa vigilance, juvenile vulnerability, and predator-response behavior.
- In the real world, moas were rapidly driven extinct after human arrival in New Zealand.
- Moa domestication or ridability is not baseline reference; it belongs in Cassowary World lore.

## Context

Moas are important to Cassowary World because they become the successful large riding and transport birds imported from New Zealand into Sahul. The real-world baseline creates both opportunities and constraints. Their size and avian development make egg capture, imprinting, and selective breeding plausible paths into domestication, but their lack of wings and uncertain social structure mean they should not be treated as naturally horse-like.

For lore purposes, female moas are the primary candidates for riding because larger females provide the most plausible body size and load capacity. This is a Cassowary World inference, not a settled baseline claim about real-world use.
