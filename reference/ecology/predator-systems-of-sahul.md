# Predator Systems of Sahul

## Summary

Real-world baseline for the predator assemblage present in Sahul during the early Pleistocene. Defines the ecological roles, predation strategies, and habitat preferences of major predators, and the constraints their presence imposed on prey species and landscape use.

## Core Reality

- Sahul supported a distinct predator guild that evolved independently from Eurasian and African predator lineages. The major predators were marsupials, reptiles, and large birds — not placental carnivores.
- *Thylacoleo carnifex* (marsupial lion — a powerful, heavily-clawed predator of large prey) was likely the apex terrestrial predator for much of the Pleistocene. Body mass estimates range from 100–160 kg. It had robust forelimbs with retractile claws and a highly specialised shearing bite that could process bone. Evidence suggests semi-arboreal capability. It was not a pursuit predator; its morphology indicates ambush or short-burst attack.
- *Varanus priscus* (Megalania — giant monitor lizard, closely related to the Komodo dragon), reached an estimated body length of 3.5–7 m and weighed several hundred kilograms. Komodo-like predation strategy: ambush, venom or septic bite, and carrion consumption. Likely occupied open and woodland habitats. A dominant predator of large herbivores.
- *Quinkana* (a genus of large semi-terrestrial crocodilians) were longer-legged than modern aquatic crocodiles and capable of hunting in terrestrial environments. They occupied zones near water but could move overland. They imposed predation risk in riparian and wetland corridors.
- *Thylacinus cynocephalus* (thylacine — a pursuit predator with wolf-like body plan) was a mesopredator specialising in smaller to medium-sized prey. It lacked the mass to reliably attack megafauna adults but imposed risk on juveniles and on medium-sized prey species.
- *Crocodylus porosus* (saltwater crocodile) and *C. johnstoni* (freshwater crocodile) were present in Sahul's waterways. Saltwater crocodiles can range well into estuarine and coastal marine zones; they imposed major predation risk at water access points.
- Large eagles, including ancestors of the extant wedge-tailed eagle (*Aquila audax*), imposed aerial predation pressure on juveniles and smaller prey across open terrain.
- The predator guild occupied distinct habitat niches with some overlap: Thylacoleo in woodland and forest margins; Megalania in open and semi-open terrain; Quinkana in riparian zones; crocodilians at water access points; thylacines in open and woodland terrain.

## Constraints

- Water access imposed predation risk from crocodilians regardless of broader terrain safety; movement to and from water sources required predator awareness, and ambush sites near water were not avoidable.
- Open terrain imposed risk from Megalania and, in some zones, Quinkana; movement through open country required threat awareness and increased risk for slower-moving individuals.
- Forest margins and dense cover imposed risk from Thylacoleo's ambush strategy; forest edge zones were not safe movement corridors.
- Predation pressure on juveniles was substantially higher than on adults for all megafauna species; juvenile survival was a critical constraint for population persistence.
- Multiple co-occurring predators with different habitat niches meant that low-risk movement required identifying which habitat transitions minimised total predator exposure, not simply avoiding one predator type.
- Nesting and denning sites for prey species required positions that minimised exposure to the full predator guild, not just one apex predator.

## System Implications

- Movement routing through Sahul landscapes required simultaneous assessment of multiple predator types occupying different habitat zones.
- Water access — essential for large animals — concentrated both prey and crocodilian predators, creating unavoidable risk chokepoints.
- Forest edge and ecotone zones, often the most productive habitats for frugivores, were also preferred zones for ambush predators; high productivity and high predation risk coincided.
- Population density of prey species was bounded not only by food availability but by the combined predation pressure of a multi-species predator guild.
- Juvenile protection was a significant constraint on spacing and movement patterns for large-bodied prey species with slow reproduction.

## Known Variability

- Body size estimates for Megalania vary considerably in the literature; the largest estimates may overstate actual size. Even conservative estimates place it as a major predator.
- Thylacoleo's hunting behaviour is inferred from skeletal morphology; direct behavioural evidence is unavailable.
- The geographic overlap between Thylacoleo, Megalania, and Quinkana varied by region; not all Sahul zones experienced the full predator guild simultaneously.
- Quinkana's terrestrial capability is debated; the degree to which it hunted away from water is uncertain.
- New Guinea's predator assemblage differed from mainland Australia's; some large terrestrial predators may not have crossed between the two regions during every land connection event.

## Open Questions

- What was the geographic range of Thylacoleo during the ~2 MYA representative window, and was it present in New Guinea?
- Did Megalania (Varanus priscus) and Thylacoleo compete directly for large prey, and how did this partition prey species across habitats?
- What was the actual body mass of large Megalania individuals, and how does this affect estimates of prey size and kill frequency?
