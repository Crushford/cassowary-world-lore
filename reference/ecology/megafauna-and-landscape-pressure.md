# Megafauna and Landscape Pressure

## Summary

Real-world baseline for how large-bodied herbivores and omnivores impose physical, ecological, and behavioural pressure on the landscapes they inhabit. Defines the mechanisms through which megafauna presence shapes vegetation, movement corridors, and available habitat for other species.

## Metadata

- Primary topic: Megafauna and landscape pressure
- Layer: Real-world reference
- Topics: megafauna, landscape pressure, browsing, trampling, megaherbivore, vegetation structure, disturbance ecology, carrying capacity
- Regions: Sahul (Australia, New Guinea)
- Related species: Diprotodon (diprotodontid — giant wombat-relative, largest known marsupial), Procoptodon (giant short-faced kangaroo — large grazing macropod), Genyornis and related dromornithids (giant flightless birds — large herbivorous or omnivorous birds), Palorchestes (palorchestid — large clawed browser), Zygomaturus (zygomaturine — semi-aquatic wombat-relative)

## Core Reality

- Large-bodied herbivores impose landscape pressure through multiple simultaneous mechanisms: browsing (selective removal of vegetation), grazing (removal of ground cover), trampling (compaction and physical destruction), wallowing (soil disturbance and water quality alteration), and high dung loads (localised nutrient concentration).
- Browsing by large animals removes woody plant material at heights accessible to the animal, shaping canopy structure and understory composition. Animals that browse at 1–2 m height prevent canopy closure and maintain open or semi-open woodland conditions.
- Trampling by large animals compacts soil, reduces seedling recruitment, and physically destroys low-growing plants. In heavily used zones — around waterholes, along regular movement routes, at sleeping or resting sites — trampling can eliminate ground cover entirely.
- Dung concentration at resting and watering sites creates nutrient hotspots that alter local plant community composition, favouring nitrophilous plants over species adapted to lower nutrient levels.
- Megaherbivores (animals over roughly 1,000 kg) consume enough vegetation that their population density directly determines the carrying capacity of the landscape; vegetation productivity bounds megaherbivore numbers before resource competition with smaller species becomes a secondary factor.
- Movement routes for large animals — between water sources, between productive vegetation patches, between seasonal habitats — become worn corridors of disturbed soil and reduced vegetation. These corridors persist in the landscape after the animals move on.
- Waterhole dependence concentrates megafauna at permanent water sources, creating zones of intense pressure around water that radiate outward from the source.
- Large body size means slow reproduction; megafauna populations respond slowly to food availability changes. Population crashes take time to recover and can produce landscape recovery lags.

## Constraints

- Vegetation zones within megafauna range cannot be assumed to persist undisturbed; browsing pressure limits canopy closure, trampling reduces seedling survival, and waterhole zones experience concentrated disturbance.
- Movement through landscapes with megafauna populations requires awareness of preferred movement routes (worn corridors), waterhole zones, and resting areas — all of which carry both disturbance and animal proximity risk.
- Productive zones occupied by megafauna are shared resources; smaller herbivores and frugivores compete with megafauna for access to the same high-quality vegetation patches.
- Megafauna presence at waterhole zones makes water access a potential conflict or risk point; large animals at water can displace or endanger smaller animals.
- Infrastructure placed in heavily used megafauna zones (waterhole perimeters, movement corridors) experiences sustained physical disturbance from trampling, wallowing, and browsing.
- Slow megafauna reproduction means populations cannot rapidly replenish after mortality; heavy predation or hunting removes individuals from the landscape for extended periods before recovery.

## System Implications

- Landscape planning must account for megafauna movement patterns and pressure zones; productive zones near water or along movement corridors experience a different disturbance regime than zones outside megafauna range.
- Vegetation management in zones with active megafauna pressure requires either exclusion of animals or management of pressure intensity; passive vegetation recovery fails under sustained megafauna load.
- Competition for high-productivity vegetation zones is unavoidable where megafauna and other herbivores co-occur; assuming exclusive access to productive patches is not a valid baseline.
- Megafauna presence shapes the mosaic of open and closed vegetation; landscapes without megafauna browse pressure would show different canopy closure patterns.

## Known Variability

- Pressure intensity varies with population density, season, and rainfall; dry-season pressure around permanent water is more intense than wet-season pressure when animals disperse.
- Different species imposed different pressure profiles; a large browser (Diprotodon) had different effects from a large grazer (Procoptodon) and both differed from a semi-aquatic wetland herbivore (Zygomaturus).
- Pressure was not uniformly distributed across the landscape; megafauna preferred specific habitat types and avoided others, creating spatial variation in disturbance regimes.
- Megafauna density varied across Sahul ecosystems; arid interior zones supported lower densities than productive wetland or monsoon woodland zones.

## Open Questions

- What was the population density of major megafauna species in productive northern Sahul zones during the ~2 MYA representative period?
- How far from permanent water did megafauna ranging zones extend during the dry season in arid and semi-arid zones?
- What was the spatial scale of trampling and disturbance zones around permanent waterholes used by Diprotodon-scale animals?
