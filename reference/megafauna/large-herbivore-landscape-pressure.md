# Large Herbivore Landscape Pressure

## Summary

Real-world baseline for the specific mechanisms through which large herbivores — particularly marsupial megaherbivores in Sahul — impose physical and ecological pressure on landscape structure, vegetation, and other species. Distinguishes browsing, grazing, trampling, and waterhole dynamics as separate mechanisms with different spatial and ecological effects.

## Metadata

- Primary topic: Large herbivore landscape pressure
- Layer: Real-world reference
- Topics: large herbivores, browsing, grazing, trampling, waterhole dynamics, vegetation structure, carrying capacity, landscape disturbance, megaherbivore
- Regions: Sahul (Australia, New Guinea)

## Core Reality

- Large herbivores impose landscape pressure through five primary mechanisms: selective browsing, grazing, trampling, wallowing, and waterhole concentration effects.
- **Browsing** (selective removal of leaves, shoots, bark, and woody stems): animals feeding at height remove plant material from specific vertical zones. An animal browsing at 1–2 m height prevents canopy closure below that height, maintaining open or semi-open conditions. Repeated browsing on the same plants inhibits recovery and can kill individuals over time. High-quality plants — palatable species — are preferentially removed, shifting plant community composition toward less palatable species.
- **Grazing** (removal of grasses and low-growing herbaceous plants): at high densities, grazing removes ground cover entirely, exposing bare soil. Reduced ground cover decreases evapotranspiration, increases soil temperature, and changes water infiltration. Grazer selectivity also shifts grass species composition toward less palatable or physically defended grasses over time.
- **Trampling**: body mass determines pressure per unit area on soil. Animals in the 1,000–2,800 kg range (Diprotodon scale) impose significant soil compaction on regularly used surfaces. Compaction reduces root penetration depth, water infiltration, and seedling emergence. Trampling at regular movement routes, watering sites, and resting areas destroys vegetation mechanically before browsers or grazers consume it.
- **Wallowing**: large herbivores wallow in wet zones to regulate temperature and reduce parasite load. Repeated wallowing at the same site creates permanent water depressions, alters soil chemistry, eliminates surrounding vegetation, and changes local hydrology. Wallow sites become persistent landscape features even after animals have moved on.
- **Waterhole concentration**: all large terrestrial animals must access water regularly (frequency increases with body size and temperature). During dry seasons, permanent water sources become concentration points. Heavy use erodes banks, muddies water, and creates zones of intense trampling extending outward from the waterhole. These zones can extend hundreds of metres in all directions around permanent water.
- Carrying capacity for large herbivores is set by two independent ceilings: food availability (calories and protein per unit area) and water availability (access to permanent or semi-permanent water within ranging distance). Both must be met simultaneously; improving one does not raise the overall ceiling if the other remains limiting.
- Slow reproduction rates (single offspring, extended juvenile dependence) mean large herbivore populations cannot rapidly recover from mortality events. Population crashes compress slowly and recover over decades, not seasons.

## Constraints

- Vegetation within sustained browsing range cannot be assumed to retain its undisturbed composition; browse-pressure removes palatable species and favours unpalatable or physically defended plants over time.
- High-quality riparian or wetland vegetation adjacent to permanent water is subject to the highest combined pressure from trampling, grazing, and waterhole concentration; it is not equivalent to the same vegetation type in ungrazed conditions.
- Trampling zones around permanent water impose a radius of disturbed, compacted, and vegetation-poor terrain that expands during dry seasons when animal concentration is highest.
- Seedling recruitment within megaherbivore range is suppressed by trampling and browsing; canopy regeneration requires either protection from pressure or access to zones beyond megaherbivore ranging distance.
- Water availability sets a hard ceiling on large herbivore density; productive vegetation zones without access to permanent water do not support sustained large herbivore presence.
- Palatable-plant depletion means that long-term high herbivore density shifts plant community composition permanently, not just temporarily, unless herbivore density drops and recovery occurs.

## System Implications

- Productive riparian and wetland zones adjacent to permanent water are simultaneously the highest-value food zones and the highest-disturbance zones for other species.
- Vegetation management in zones with active megaherbivore populations requires accounting for the combined effect of all five pressure mechanisms, not just browsing.
- Carrying capacity calculations that ignore water access underestimate the true constraint; food availability alone does not set the ceiling.
- Long-term vegetation management under megaherbivore pressure must plan for palatable-species depletion and community composition shift.

## Known Variability

- Pressure intensity varies seasonally; dry-season waterhole concentration effects are more intense than wet-season dispersed grazing pressure.
- Different species imposed different pressure profiles; Diprotodon (browser) and large macropod grazers produced different vegetation effects even at similar body masses.
- Wetland megaherbivores (Zygomaturus) imposed different pressure on aquatic and semi-aquatic vegetation than dryland browsers imposed on woodland vegetation.
- Population density varied across Sahul habitats; arid zones supported much lower densities than monsoon woodland or wetland zones.
