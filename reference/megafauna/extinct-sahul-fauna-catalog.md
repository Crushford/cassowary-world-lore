# Extinct Sahul Fauna Catalog

## Summary

A structured catalog of major extinct Sahul fauna, with ecological role, body size, and constraint implications for each. Covers herbivores, predators, and large birds present during the early Pleistocene window relevant to this lore setting. Entries include uncertainty notes where the ~2 MYA presence of specific taxa is unresolved.

- Regions: Sahul (Australia, New Guinea)

## Core Reality

Entries are ordered by ecological role. The ~2 MYA representative window is a snapshot; not all species listed have confirmed presence at exactly 2 MYA — see Known Variability.

### Large Browsers and Grazers

**Diprotodon optatum** (Diprotodon — the largest known marsupial, a rhinoceros-sized browser and browser-grazer)
- Body mass: estimated 1,000–2,800 kg; the largest individuals may have exceeded cattle-sized animals.
- Ecology: browser and mixed feeder on woody vegetation, shrubs, and chenopod plants. Evidence of seasonal movement between habitats. Waterhole dependent.
- Constraint: imposed the highest single-animal vegetation consumption load of any Sahul herbivore; populations required large productive ranges and permanent water access.

**Zygomaturus trilobus** (Zygomaturus — a large semi-aquatic wombat-relative)
- Body mass: estimated 500–700 kg.
- Ecology: associated with wetland and floodplain margins; likely grazed aquatic and semi-aquatic vegetation. Shovel-like incisors suggest rooting or pulling of wetland plants.
- Constraint: specialised to wetland and riparian zones; presence indicates productive wet margins but does not indicate general dryland grazing capacity.

**Palorchestes azael** (Palorchestes — a large clawed marsupial browser)
- Body mass: estimated 200–250 kg.
- Ecology: large retractile claws indicate specialist browsing on specific vegetation types, possibly bark-stripping, root-digging, or pulling down branches. Not a grazer.
- Constraint: clawed manipulation of vegetation created a distinct disturbance type — physical tearing and pulling of woody plants — different from bite-browsing.

**Procoptodon goliah** (Procoptodon — the largest known kangaroo)
- Body mass: estimated 200–240 kg.
- Ecology: large short-faced kangaroo with specialised teeth for processing tough or low-nutrient vegetation. Grazed and browsed across open and semi-open habitats.
- Constraint: presence in open terrain imposed competition pressure for ground-cover vegetation. *Note: fossil record for Procoptodon is predominantly from later Pleistocene; ~2 MYA presence is not confirmed. Use with uncertainty.*

### Large Flightless Birds

**Genyornis newtoni** (Genyornis — a giant dromornithid flightless bird)
- Body mass: estimated 200–250 kg.
- Ecology: herbivorous or omnivorous; large bill suited to processing tough plant material, seeds, or fruit. Not a predator. See [Dromornithidae](dromornithidae.md) for full lineage context.
- Constraint: imposed sustained vegetation pressure as a large-bodied ground bird. *Note: Genyornis fossil record is predominantly ~700 kya to ~40 kya; earlier dromornithid representatives (Ilbandornis, Bullockornis) are more appropriate for the ~2 MYA window.*

### Predators

**Thylacoleo carnifex** (marsupial lion — apex ambush predator)
- Body mass: estimated 100–160 kg.
- Ecology: powerful forelimbs with retractile claws, highly specialised shearing bite. Ambush or short-burst attack strategy. Possibly semi-arboreal. Occupied woodland and forest margin habitats. See [Predator Systems of Sahul](../ecology/predator-systems-of-sahul.md).
- Constraint: apex terrestrial predator; its presence in woodland-forest edge zones imposed unavoidable predation risk on large prey species including megafauna juveniles.

**Varanus priscus** (Megalania — giant monitor lizard, related to Komodo dragon)
- Body mass: highly debated; conservative estimates 100–200 kg, extreme estimates up to 1,900 kg. Even conservative estimates make it the largest terrestrial lizard known.
- Ecology: ambush predator, venomous bite (inferred from Komodo relationship), opportunistic carrion consumer. Open and semi-open habitats.
- Constraint: Komodo-model predation risk in open terrain and at carcass sites; juveniles and smaller animals at highest risk from Megalania-style ambush.

**Quinkana fortirostrum** (Quinkana — giant semi-terrestrial crocodilian)
- Body mass: rough estimates several hundred kg; body length 2–3+ m.
- Ecology: longer-legged than aquatic crocodiles; capable of terrestrial movement beyond water margins. Associated with riparian and wetland zones but not strictly aquatic.
- Constraint: imposed predation risk in riparian corridors that extended beyond the immediate waterline; not safely avoidable by simply staying clear of open water.

### New Guinea Giant Insectivores

**Zaglossus** species (giant long-beaked echidna — large insectivore-earthworm specialist)
- Body mass: estimated 5–15 kg depending on species (substantially larger than modern echidnas).
- Ecology: long-beaked echidna relatives specialised for probing soil and leaf litter for earthworms and invertebrates. Present in New Guinea and previously in Australia.
- Constraint: ecosystem indicator; their presence indicates sufficient invertebrate-rich soil layers to support a specialised large consumer.

## Constraints

- The full catalog cannot be assumed to represent all species present at any single locality or time within the early Pleistocene window; local assemblages varied by habitat and region.
- Several catalog entries (Procoptodon, Genyornis) have uncertain ~2 MYA presence; the ~2 MYA window may represent earlier relatives, not the named species.
- Size estimates for large-bodied taxa involve substantial uncertainty; constraint implications are robust to this uncertainty but specific mass figures should not be treated as precise.
- The predator catalog and herbivore catalog represent co-occurring species; predation pressure affected megafauna juveniles and smaller individuals, not only smaller prey species.

## System Implications

- This catalog defines the baseline megafauna guild that Sahul productive zones had to support; resource planning must account for combined consumption loads of multiple large-bodied species.
- Predator presence at multiple ecological levels (apex predator, large monitor, semi-terrestrial crocodilian) created a layered predation risk landscape across terrain types.
- The combination of large herbivore consumption and large predator risk meant that productive zones were simultaneously high in food value and high in predation exposure.

## Known Variability

- Regional assemblages varied; not all taxa were present across all of Sahul simultaneously. New Guinea highlands had different assemblages from northern Australian lowlands.
- Extinction timing for individual taxa varied; the ~40 kya megafauna extinction event removed most of these species, but the early Pleistocene window is well before that event.
- Fossil record gaps mean some taxa may have been present at ~2 MYA but are not yet documented; the catalog represents known taxa, not necessarily a complete inventory.

## Open Questions

- Which dromornithid genera were present in northern Sahul during the ~2 MYA representative window?
- What was the actual body mass of large Megalania individuals, and how does this affect prey size estimates?
- Was Procoptodon or a direct ancestor present at ~2 MYA, or is this lineage better represented by earlier macropod taxa?
