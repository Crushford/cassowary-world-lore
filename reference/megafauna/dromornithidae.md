# Dromornithidae

Layer: Real-world reference
Status: Draft

## Summary

This file records baseline information about Dromornithidae, the extinct giant flightless birds of Australia and Sahul. They are relevant to Cassowary World because they form part of the megafauna context in which cassowary societies develop, especially in open woodland, floodplain, lake-margin, and inland environments.

## Atomic Notes

- Dromornithids were extinct giant flightless birds from Australia and Sahul, often called mihirungs or thunder birds.
- Dromornithids were not ratites and were not close relatives of moas, cassowaries, emus, or ostriches.
- Dromornithids are generally treated as close relatives of waterfowl within or near the broader duck, goose, swan, and screamer lineage.
- Important dromornithid genera include `Dromornis`, `Bullockornis`, `Genyornis`, and `Ilbandornis`.
- Some dromornithids were among the largest known birds by body mass.
- Diet interpretation has varied historically; a cautious baseline treats them as large herbivorous or omnivorous birds rather than confirmed specialized apex predators.
- Dromornithids may shape Sahul landscapes through browsing, trampling, nesting disturbance, competition, and dangerous territorial behavior.
- Adult dromornithids would be difficult prey for many predators, while eggs, chicks, juveniles, injured adults, and isolated individuals would be more vulnerable.
- Dromornithids are useful reference context for cassowary cultural knowledge about giant birds, egg capture, dangerous megafauna, and prestige handling.

## Context

Dromornithids give Sahul a native giant-bird megafauna context distinct from New Zealand moas. They should not be treated as interchangeable with moas. Moas are New Zealand ratites; dromornithids are Australian waterfowl relatives with a different evolutionary history, body plan, and likely ecological role.

The popular image of a carnivorous "demon duck" should be used carefully. Some older interpretations emphasized predatory potential, but current cautious reference use should avoid making dromornithids confirmed terror-bird analogues unless a separate Cassowary World divergence or lore document explicitly justifies that claim.

For Cassowary World, dromornithids are most useful as large, dangerous, culturally significant animals that cassowary societies encounter in Sahul. They may be competitors, hazards, egg sources, prestige animals, or rare handling candidates, but any domestication or riding claims belong in lore documents rather than this reference baseline.
