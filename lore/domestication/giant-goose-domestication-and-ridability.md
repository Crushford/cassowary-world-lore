# Giant Goose Domestication and Ridability

## Summary

This document explains how some Australian giant goose lineages could move from egg capture and chick rearing into true domestication, limited load use, eventual ridability, and regional mounted warfare. It treats the pathway as a canon-candidate refinement of existing dromornithid handling logic: cassowaries are unusually capable bird domesticators because their own reproduction already depends on controlled fire incubation, egg management, chick emergence, imprinting, and long juvenile care.

The claim is not that all dromornithids become domestic animals. The claim is that some Australian anseriform giant birds, if ecologically accessible and behaviourally suitable, are more plausible domestication targets than wild adult megafauna because cassowary households can begin control at the egg stage.

## Atomic Notes

- Cassowary fire use begins as a survival and food-processing behaviour before it becomes a mature domestic technology.
- Fire improves digestibility and food reliability, giving it a broad civilizational role similar to cooking in human societies.
- Fire also changes cassowary reproduction by making warmer, more stable egg incubation possible in managed shelters.
- Over evolutionary and cultural time, intelligent cassowary eggs become dependent on warmer and more stable incubation than unmanaged nests reliably provide.
- Fire shelters, controlled warmth, humidity management, egg turning, nest protection, chick emergence, and long juvenile care become normal cassowary household knowledge.
- This mature incubation culture exists before large bird domestication.
- Once cassowaries are expert egg managers, eggs of other bird species become plausible management targets.
- Australian giant geese are geographically easier targets than moas because they can be encountered within Sahul and do not require New Zealand contact.
- Egg capture matters more than adult capture because eggs can be incubated, hatched, and socially shaped before dangerous adult behaviour develops.
- Imprinting is the key bridge between wild giant goose biology and cassowary handling.
- Captive breeding selects for calmness, handler tolerance, flock manageability, route tolerance, and load tolerance.
- Ridability develops gradually from handler familiarity, pack use, harness experiments, route work, and multi-generation selection.
- Some ridable giant goose lineages become military mounts in regions where breeding, route infrastructure, handler training, and feed access are stable enough to support them.
- Wild adult giant geese are not automatically rideable and remain dangerous.
- Not all giant goose species, populations, or managed lineages become rideable.
- Giant goose mounted warfare is regional and lineage-specific. It is not automatic dromornithid cavalry.
- Fire incubation enables chick rearing, but it does not erase ecological, behavioural, size, or terrain constraints.

## Context

This document depends on [Fire Incubation and Extended Juvenile Development](../biology/fire-incubation-and-extended-juvenile-development.md). Cassowary households already know how to keep eggs warm, stable, protected, turned, and humid enough to hatch. They already know how to manage chicks that emerge into a domestic setting and require extended feeding, shelter, and social learning.

That matters because cassowaries are not humans trying to domesticate birds from outside an avian life cycle. They are large birds whose own reproductive system has become culturally and biologically entangled with managed incubation. Their domestic expertise begins with nests, eggs, warmth, humidity, shelter, emergence, and juvenile behaviour. Large bird domestication is an extension of that existing technical world.

The relevant real-world baseline comes from [Dromornithidae](../../reference/megafauna/dromornithidae.md) and [Extinct Sahul Fauna Catalog](../../reference/megafauna/extinct-sahul-fauna-catalog.md). Dromornithids were giant flightless birds within or near the waterfowl lineage. They are commonly called mihirungs or thunder birds, and their specific early-Pleistocene genus assignment remains unresolved. This document therefore uses "giant goose" as a functional shorthand for suitable Australian anseriform giant bird lineages, not as a claim that every named dromornithid genus is available or domesticable in every period.

This pathway is geographically easier than [Moa Domestication and Ridability](../transport/moa-domestication-and-ridability.md). Moa domestication requires New Zealand arrival and a long isolated handling tradition before regular Sahul contact. Giant goose domestication can begin inside Sahul wherever cassowary communities overlap with suitable giant bird nesting grounds. It does not need a maritime contact precondition.

## Domestication Pathway

The pathway begins with cassowary fire use.

Fire makes food more digestible, extends usable food categories, warms occupied spaces, deters some predators, and supports longer juvenile dependency. The same fire culture also produces controlled incubation spaces. Once cassowary reproduction depends on managed warmth, cassowary households accumulate practical knowledge of egg condition, shell handling, turning, humidity, bedding, shelter, chick emergence, feeding, and protection.

That knowledge creates the bridge to other birds.

Adult giant geese are poor first targets. They are large, defensive, and already shaped by wild threat responses. Egg capture is more plausible. Eggs can be taken opportunistically, salvaged from disturbed nests, or captured during managed raids on nesting grounds. Captured eggs can be placed into existing fire-incubation shelters rather than requiring a new technology.

Hatched chicks enter cassowary social space from the beginning.

Chicks raised around cassowary handlers do not become fully cassowary, but imprinting and early familiarity reduce the distance between wild response and managed response. A chick that learns handler presence, feeding routines, shelter routines, and route movement from hatching is easier to manage than an adult taken from the wild. This is the same broad logic that later makes moa domestication plausible, but it can begin earlier and closer to cassowary core territories.

True domestication requires captive breeding.

First-generation handled birds are not domestic lineages. They are managed individuals. Domestication begins only when handlers can keep enough birds alive, pair or group them reliably, protect nesting sites, hatch the next generation, and select which birds reproduce. Selection favours calmness near cassowaries, tolerance for touch and close approach, reduced panic, flock manageability, predictable feeding, tolerance for route movement, and gradual load tolerance.

Ridability is a late outcome.

Load-bearing begins with tolerated contact, carried bundles, short supervised movement, and route familiarity. Harness systems and road or trail routines then select for birds that remain calm under pressure, recover from stress, and move predictably with handlers. Riding, where it develops, emerges after generations of selection and training. It is not a property of wild adults and not a universal property of all managed lineages.

Mounted warfare is later and narrower than riding.

A bird that can carry a handler on a known route is not automatically a war mount. Military use requires tolerance for noise, crowding, sudden movement, blood scent, injured animals, unfamiliar terrain, and close approach by other mounted birds. The earliest military role is likely courier work, scout movement, intimidation, and elite shock display along known corridors. Battlefield use expands only where lineages remain controllable under stress and where institutions can maintain handlers, replacement birds, route access, and feed.

Giant goose mounted warfare is therefore real but regional. It emerges first in societies that already have suitable lineages, nesting access, handler traditions, and terrain that rewards fast ground movement. It does not create uniform continental cavalry. It produces local mounted forces with strong prestige value, high maintenance costs, and uneven tactical reliability.

This differs from dromornithid prestige handling.

[Dromornithid Prestige Handling](dromornithid-prestige-handling.md) describes rare egg capture, chick rearing, and elite display without assuming routine transport or full domestication. Giant goose domestication is a narrower and stronger claim: it requires sustained breeding populations, heritable selection, handler institutions, and functional uses beyond ceremonial display. If a lineage never reaches captive breeding and stable trait selection, it remains prestige handling or semi-domestication rather than true domestication.

## Social Consequences

Managed giant geese create new specialist roles before they create transport systems.

Egg scouts, nest raiders, incubation specialists, chick feeders, route handlers, and breeding-lineage keepers all become valuable because each stage can fail. The knowledge is practical rather than symbolic: handlers must know when an egg is viable, how warm it can be kept without damage, how chicks respond to feeding and shelter, which juveniles tolerate proximity, and which adults are too dangerous to keep near settlements.

Successful lineages can become prestige animals, pack animals, route animals, limited mounts, or military mounts depending on body size, temperament, diet, terrain, and available infrastructure. A calm smaller lineage may be useful for prestige and light load work but unsuitable for riding. A larger lineage may carry more but require wider routes, more feed, stronger handlers, and stricter breeding control.

Mounted giant geese change warfare before they standardise it.

They allow rapid movement between known nodes, mounted scouting, message carrying, elite raiding, pursuit of disordered enemies, and intimidation in political theatre. They also create new vulnerabilities. A frightened mount can injure its rider, break formation, or trigger panic in nearby birds. Forces that use them need specialist handlers, rest points, feed planning, and terrain knowledge. Their military value is high where these supports exist and low where they do not.

The system also changes later moa domestication.

By the time cassowaries reach New Zealand or exchange knowledge with New Zealand communities, they do not arrive with only abstract curiosity about large birds. They already possess an incubation and selection toolkit: egg control, chick imprinting, captive breeding, handler familiarity, route training, harness experimentation, and limited mounted warfare. Moa domestication still has its own New Zealand constraints, but it is easier because the general large-bird management pathway already exists.

This also explains why moas remain valuable after giant goose warfare exists. Giant goose mounts are Sahul-native but unevenly distributed, lineage-specific, and tied to local nesting grounds and ecological constraints. Moa lineages develop separately in New Zealand and may offer different size, temperament, endurance, terrain, or institutional advantages. Moa import does not replace giant goose mounted warfare; it adds another mount tradition with a different logistical and military niche.

Diprotodontid haulage adds a third transport category. Giant geese are faster regional mounts and military animals where selected lineages exist. Diprotodontids are slow heavy-haul animals for bulk loads, road economics, and tribute movement. The two systems complement each other rather than competing for the same role.

## Limits

- Not all giant goose species or lineages become rideable.
- Wild adult giant geese remain dangerous and are not assumed to be safe transport animals.
- Egg capture is easier than adult control, but egg capture still requires access to nesting grounds and exposes handlers to adult defence.
- Fire incubation enables chick rearing but does not remove ecological constraints, feed requirements, disease risk, aggression, or terrain limits.
- First-generation handled birds are not equivalent to domesticated lineages.
- True domestication requires repeated captive breeding and selection across generations.
- Ridability must emerge gradually through load tolerance, route tolerance, harness tolerance, and handler tolerance.
- Mounted warfare requires additional selection for stress tolerance, formation tolerance, wound tolerance, and recovery after panic.
- Giant geese do not become horse-equivalents. Their gait, heat tolerance, feeding needs, stress responses, social behaviour, and ecological range remain avian and lineage-specific.
- Giant goose warfare remains most plausible as regional mounted scouting, courier movement, raiding, pursuit, elite shock display, and limited battlefield use, not universal heavy cavalry.
- Giant geese are not heavy haulage animals. Bulk tribute and road-based freight belong to diprotodontid systems where those animals can be supported.
- Dromornithid prestige handling, semi-domestication, ceremonial control, and true domestication are separate categories unless future reference work supports merging them.
- No named dromornithid genus should be treated as the default rideable giant goose until the reference-layer species and timeline questions are resolved.

## Open Questions

- Which dromornithid or Australian anseriform giant bird lineages are small, calm, or social enough for true domestication rather than prestige handling?
- Which Sahul regions provide enough overlap between cassowary settlements and suitable giant bird nesting grounds?
- When does giant goose domestication begin relative to moa domestication in New Zealand?
- How long does selection need to produce reliable load-bearing or riding lineages?
- Which uses emerge first: prestige display, pack transport, route work, or riding?
- Which military uses emerge first: courier work, scouting, raiding, pursuit, elite shock display, or battlefield formation?
- Does giant goose management remain regional, or does it spread through specialist handler lineages and exchange networks?
