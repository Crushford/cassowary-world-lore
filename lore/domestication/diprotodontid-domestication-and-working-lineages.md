# Diprotodontid Domestication and Working Lineages

## Summary

This document explains how diprotodontid management develops from long-term observation of wild megafauna into taming, partial breeding control, and eventually heavy working lineages. It treats diprotodontids as slow, powerful, expensive infrastructure animals whose economic role emerges across eras rather than as instantly domesticated livestock.

The core pathway is: repeated non-predatory proximity -> landscape knowledge -> juvenile protection and habituation -> individual taming -> limited work -> controlled breeding -> road-based haulage and redistribution.

## Atomic Notes

- Diprotodontid domestication begins with observation, not capture.
- Early cassowaries do not impose systematic hunting pressure because their food base is primarily fruit, preserved sugar, and managed forest resources.
- Repeated non-predatory proximity lets cassowaries learn diprotodontid movement, waterhole timing, feeding routes, and stress signals.
- Juvenile intervention creates the behavioural bridge, but it is slower and less reliable than bird imprinting.
- Early managed animals are tamed individuals, not domestic lineages.
- Diprotodontid work begins before controlled breeding, through restrained and trained individuals used for clearing, dragging, and path work.
- True domestication begins only when calmer individuals can be selected and bred across generations.
- Diprotodontid working lineages are slow, powerful, water-dependent, expensive, and institutionally valuable.
- Heavy haulage in the Early History Era makes durable roads economically worthwhile.
- Political control over trained animals, breeding herds, handlers, and water access becomes economic power.
- Diprotodontids are not cavalry, fast transport, primary food animals, or household livestock.
- Loss of trained animals disrupts transport, tribute movement, and regional redistribution because they cannot be quickly replaced.

## Context

The reference baseline comes from [Extinct Sahul Fauna Catalog](../../reference/megafauna/extinct-sahul-fauna-catalog.md) and [Megafauna and Landscape Pressure](../../reference/ecology/megafauna-and-landscape-pressure.md). Diprotodontid-grade animals were large marsupial herbivores that imposed browsing, trampling, waterhole, and movement-corridor pressure across productive Sahul landscapes. They were not small domesticates waiting to be owned. They were landscape-scale animals whose presence already shaped route choice, water access, vegetation, and settlement risk.

This pathway differs from bird domestication. Cassowaries can manage giant goose or moa eggs through fire incubation and imprinting. Diprotodontids are mammals. They cannot be brought under control through egg capture, and juveniles depend on maternal care. The bridge is therefore repeated familiarity, juvenile protection, opportunistic adoption, gradual habituation, and long individual training.

The pathway also differs from ordinary hunting. Cassowary societies are not driven by a meat-first economy. Their core surplus systems come from orchards, honeypot ants, preserved sugar, and ceramic storage. This lowers predation pressure on diprotodontids and allows long-term observation to accumulate before intervention.

## Domestication Timeline

### Stage 1 — Wild Observation Phase

Time period: Early [Fire and Extended Development Era](../../generated/time/fire-and-extended-development-era.md).

Cassowaries observe wild diprotodontids at waterholes, feeding zones, disturbed corridors, and forest-edge browsing patches. The relationship is not yet management. It is repeated exposure to large herbivore behaviour.

No systematic hunting pressure develops at this stage. Diprotodontids are large, dangerous, slow to reproduce, and not aligned with the cassowary fruit-and-sugar food base. Cassowaries gain more by learning where these animals move than by treating them as ordinary prey.

Key idea: cassowaries learn behaviour before attempting control.

### Stage 2 — Tolerated Proximity and Landscape Awareness

Time period: Late [Fire and Extended Development Era](../../generated/time/fire-and-extended-development-era.md).

Cassowaries begin predicting diprotodontid waterhole cycles, feeding routes, resting zones, and movement corridors. Familiar animals may tolerate cassowary presence when cassowaries are not acting as predators and do not block access to water or forage.

Diprotodontid corridors become known landscape features. Their paths indicate water, seasonal vegetation, disturbance zones, and safe or unsafe movement times. This knowledge informs route choice before it becomes animal management.

Key idea: knowledge precedes intervention.

### Stage 3 — Juvenile Intervention and Protection

Time period: Early [Ecological Management Era](../../generated/time/ecological-management-era.md).

Cassowaries begin intervening around vulnerable juveniles. Intervention may include driving off smaller predators, protecting isolated young near water, guiding displaced juveniles away from danger, or opportunistically capturing juveniles after disturbance, maternal death, or herd separation.

This is the mammalian equivalent of an imprinting bridge, but it is slower and less reliable than bird imprinting. Juvenile diprotodontids already have maternal dependence, fear responses, and species-specific social expectations. Habituation requires repeated feeding, calm proximity, restraint tolerance, and protection over long periods.

Juvenile intervention is most plausible with older pouch-independent juveniles, not pouch young or highly dependent newborns. Pouch young and newborns require continuous maternal physiology that cassowaries cannot replicate, making survival rates negligible and intervention biologically implausible at that stage.

Key idea: juvenile familiarity creates the first behavioural bridge, but it does not create domestication.

### Stage 4 — Managed Taming and Individual Training

Time period: [Ecological Management Era](../../generated/time/ecological-management-era.md).

Individual diprotodontids are restrained, guided, fed near managed sites, and trained over long periods. This resembles early elephant-taming logic more than household livestock domestication. Handlers work with individuals whose behaviour can become predictable enough for specific tasks.

There is no true breeding control yet. A trained animal may be valuable, but its offspring are not reliably controlled, and its temperament is not yet a stable lineage trait. Handler knowledge matters more than heredity.

Key idea: this is taming, not full domestication.

### Stage 5 — Early Working Use

Time period: Late [Ecological Management Era](../../generated/time/ecological-management-era.md).

Tamed diprotodontids begin limited work roles near settlements, orchards, waterholes, and maintained corridors. They drag logs, pull deadwood from orchard edges, remove fallen trees, clear heavy brush, trample or stabilize rough paths, and open space around storage or gathering sites.

Work emerges before breeding systems. The first economic value is local force: one trained animal can move material that many cassowaries cannot move efficiently by body strength alone.

Key idea: practical work precedes true breeding control.

### Stage 6 — Controlled Breeding Lineages

Time period: [Protohistoric Expansion Era](../../generated/time/protohistoric-expansion-era.md).

Cassowaries begin partially controlling reproduction by protecting calmer animals, keeping trained individuals near known sites, managing access to water and forage, and preferentially retaining juveniles from tolerant mothers. Breeding is not total at first, but selection pressure becomes directional.

Working lineages emerge with reduced aggression, higher tolerance of handlers, better response to guidance, predictable stress behaviour, and greater ability to work around routes, storage nodes, and other animals.

True domestication begins here because temperament and work tolerance become lineage traits rather than only individual training outcomes.

Key idea: domestication begins when breeding and selection become durable across generations.

### Stage 7 — Integrated Transport and Infrastructure System

Time period: [Early History Era](../../generated/time/early-history-era.md).

Diprotodontids become core economic infrastructure in regions that can support them. They move heavy goods, pull bulk loads, haul ceramic tribute vessels, support road-based transport, and make long-distance redistribution more reliable.

They are slow but powerful. They require water access, feed planning, rest points, trained handlers, maintained routes, and institutional protection. They are expensive and valuable enough that ownership, breeding rights, and access to working animals become political questions.

Diprotodontids make roads economically viable. Roads reduce injury, energy cost, load loss, handler risk, and seasonal failure for heavy animals. Once haulage animals exist, route maintenance becomes an investment with measurable returns rather than only a convenience for foot traffic.

Key idea: diprotodontids turn roads from useful corridors into economic infrastructure.

## Social and Economic Role

In the [Early History Era](../../generated/time/early-history-era.md), diprotodontids function as heavy working animals. They are roughly equivalent in economic role to oxen as draft power, but larger, slower, more expensive, more dangerous, and more tightly constrained by water and forage.

They pull loads, drag timber, move ceramic tribute vessels, haul preserved food between storage nodes, and support WTA-style trade systems where bulk goods move through predictable routes and exchange points. Their value is not speed. Their value is moving more weight than cassowary bodies, hand-carried vessels, or mounted birds can move.

This changes [Transport Networks](../infrastructure/transport-networks.md). Maintained paths already matter in earlier eras, but heavy haulage makes road investment more valuable. Better surfaces, graded approaches, water access, rest nodes, and chokepoint control all increase the output of working animals.

Diprotodontids also change storage and tribute. Standardized vessels are still counted and sealed, but bulk movement allows more vessels to be forwarded to regional centers. Centralized storage becomes more practical when heavy animals can move containers, timber, sealants, and preserved food over maintained routes.

## Constraints

- Diprotodontids reproduce slowly. Population or working-lineage losses recover over decades, not seasons.
- They have high resource cost in forage, water, handler time, and route support.
- Water dependency limits where they can live, work, and travel.
- Large body size makes them dangerous to handlers, bystanders, storage sites, and route infrastructure.
- Not all Sahul regions support them. Arid interiors, steep terrain, dense wet forest, and unstable seasonal routes may be unsuitable.
- They cannot be treated as mass livestock.
- They cannot be casually replaced if killed, stolen, injured, overworked, or lost to disease.
- Work use can damage vegetation, compact soil, and degrade waterholes if management fails.
- Handler knowledge is scarce and politically valuable.
- Captive breeding requires enough animals, safe holding areas, water control, and long institutional continuity.

## System Implications

- Roads become more valuable once heavy transport exists.
- Chokepoints become more important because they can control animals, loads, water access, and route passage.
- Political control over working animals becomes economic power.
- Breeding rights, handler lineages, water access, and route maintenance become linked institutions.
- Loss of working animals disrupts trade networks, tribute forwarding, road maintenance, and redistribution.
- Heavy haulage makes larger storage centers more viable by reducing the cost of moving sealed vessels and construction materials.
- Regional inequality increases because only some ecologies can support diprotodontid breeding and haulage.
- Route authorities can tax or restrict access by controlling water, rest nodes, bridges, and maintained surfaces.

## Limits

- Diprotodontids are not cavalry.
- They are not fast transport.
- They are not primarily food animals.
- They are not widely owned at household level.
- They do not remove the need for cassowary-carried local transport, giant goose mounts, moa mounts, or river and coastal movement.
- They do not make roads appear instantly. They make road maintenance more economically valuable after earlier route systems already exist.
- They do not scale infinitely. Water, forage, slow reproduction, disease, and handler scarcity keep numbers limited.
