# Orchard Sugar Tree Lineages

## Summary

This document defines tree roles used in honeydew and sugar production without overcommitting to exact species. Cassowary orchard sugar production depends on functional tree roles rather than one universal crop species. Different regions use different tree lineages to preserve the same functions.

## Atomic Notes

- Tree choice controls honeydew flow. The choice of host tree is the upstream variable in the whole orchard-ant-tribute chain.
- Stable flow matters more than maximum flow. A tree that supports predictable moderate sap-feeder populations is more valuable than one that allows a spike and then declines.
- Aphid and sap-feeder overgrowth can damage and kill host trees. Managers must balance infestation density against tree health.
- Regional substitutions can preserve orchard function even when exact species differ. The functional role is what matters; the species name is secondary.
- Grafting and propagation preserve functional traits across generations when seed propagation does not reliably pass on those traits.
- Tree lineages become technical and possibly institutional knowledge. Knowing which trees to propagate, how, and where is expert knowledge with economic and political value.
- Long tree maturation cycles mean orchard investment is multigenerational. Trees planted in one generation may reach full productivity in the next.
- Tree loss — from disease, climate, over-infestation, or political disruption — is a long-cycle setback, not a short-cycle problem.

## Functional Tree Classes

### Sap-Flow Host Trees

The core honeydew trees.

These trees are managed primarily for honeydew flow. They are not necessarily important as fruit trees, but they are essential to the orchard-ant sugar chain.

**Traits selected for:**
- Reliable and substantial sap flow through seasonal cycles.
- Tolerance for repeated sap-feeder feeding without rapid decline.
- Recovery capacity after feeding pressure is reduced.
- Compatibility with local sap-feeder lineages — not all trees and aphids form productive partnerships.

**Management considerations:**
- Must not be overloaded. Sacrifice trees or rotation strategies protect core sap-flow hosts from too-dense sap-feeder populations.
- Recovery periods are essential. Continuous heavy infestation kills even tolerant trees.
- May need nurse canopy to stabilise microclimate.

**Civilisational role:** The foundation of cluster-level honeydew production. Without viable sap-flow host trees, the rest of the system has no fuel.

---

### Fruit-and-Sap Dual Trees

Elite mixed-use orchard trees.

These trees produce cassowary-edible fruit while also supporting sap-feeder populations on foliage, bark, or new growth. They are high-value trees with high management demands.

**Traits selected for:**
- Substantial edible fruit yield.
- Sufficient foliage and sap to support moderate sap-feeder populations without sacrificing fruit production.
- Resilience — managers must maintain a balance between fruit production and aphid pressure.

**Management considerations:**
- Balance is everything. Heavy sap-feeder loading to increase honeydew at the cost of fruit output defeats the purpose.
- These trees require the most attentive management decisions in the orchard.
- Their value makes them candidates for grafted lineage management to preserve productive individuals.

**Civilisational role:** High-prestige trees associated with elite groves and productive household orchards. Their dual utility makes them economically important beyond single-purpose trees.

---

### Nurse Canopy Trees

Orchard microclimate stabilisers.

Nurse canopy trees stabilise the orchard environment rather than directly producing sugar or fruit. They provide shade, soil moisture, wind buffering, and nest-zone microclimate protection, especially important in wet-margin and upland systems.

**Traits selected for:**
- Canopy density for shade without excessive competition for soil resources.
- Tolerance for wet, dry, or marginal conditions depending on system.
- Root structure compatible with nearby nest architecture and sap-flow host roots.

**Management considerations:**
- Nurse trees must not crowd out productive trees or reduce air circulation so much that fungal risk increases.
- May need to be pruned, managed, or rotated.
- Their value is invisible in direct output terms but visible in the survival and stability of the trees and nests they shelter.

**Civilisational role:** Infrastructure trees. Their value is understood by experienced orchard managers but may be underestimated by outside tribute collectors or administrators unfamiliar with the system.

---

### Boundary and Sacrifice Trees

Tough trees that protect core orchards from sap-feeder overflow.

These trees are planted on orchard edges, in managed belts, or in zones designed to absorb heavy sap-feeder loading. They redirect sap-feeder populations away from core fruit and sap-flow host trees.

**Traits selected for:**
- High tolerance for heavy and sustained sap-feeder infestation.
- Ability to survive extreme loading even if they decline slowly.
- Willingness to be replaced — managers expect periodic sacrifice tree loss.

**Management considerations:**
- Sacrifice trees are intentionally expendable. Their loss is planned, not a failure.
- Replacement cycles must be anticipated in orchard planning.
- Boundary placement is a management decision based on prevailing sap-feeder pressure and wind or movement patterns.

**Civilisational role:** Loss-prevention infrastructure. Without sacrifice trees, core productive trees suffer from overflow infestation during high-density sap-feeder periods.

---

### Grafted Lineage Trees

Selected and propagated for predictable functional traits.

Grafted lineage trees are not a separate ecological class — they can overlap with any of the above. They are defined by their propagation history: these are trees whose traits have been selectively preserved and transmitted through grafting, cuttings, or other vegetative propagation methods.

**Traits preserved through propagation:**
- Predictable sap timing compatible with local ant harvest schedules.
- Sap-feeder compatibility — specific trees may work better with specific local sap-feeder lineages.
- Graft survival and compatibility with regional rootstock.
- Regional climate and soil adaptation.
- Fruit quality and yield where relevant.

**Social and institutional dimensions:**
- Their value is remembered across households, groves, lineages, or institutions.
- Knowledge of which trees to propagate and how is expertise.
- Grafted lineage trees are linked to property, inheritance, and orchard continuity as an institution.
- Loss of a productive lineage — through tree death, institutional collapse, or knowledge loss — is a long-term setback.

**Civilisational role:** The most institutionally valuable tree class. They represent accumulated ecological and technical investment across generations.
