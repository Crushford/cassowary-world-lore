# Cassowary Naming and Kinship Terms

## Summary

In any project where a non-human species becomes the speaking people, a naming problem emerges. "Cassowary" describes a biological family with several living species across Sahul and nearby regions. In Cassowary World, the speaking lineage descends from a southern cassowary population and in common usage "cassowary" means the speaking people. This creates ambiguity when referring to non-speaking cassowary relatives who are biologically close but did not inherit the manipulation, language, and cognitive pathways the speaking lineage developed across millions of years of gradual divergence.

The solution is not to rename the speaking people. "Cassowary" remains the correct and intended project-facing name. The solution is to establish qualified terms for non-speaking relatives and to keep biological and cultural registers distinct. Ordinary language frequently narrows broad category terms into specific group names without biological precision — the same process by which any people-name narrows from a wider category through historical use. The speaking lineage does this to "cassowary" not through declaration but through accumulated cultural habit: the term gradually narrows around the lineage that speaks, builds, and is spoken about, while close relatives become qualified by ecology or kinship.

The naming system has two registers. In common speech and narrative, "cassowary" means the speaking people; non-speaking relatives receive qualified common names — "forest cassowaries", "dwarf forest cassowary", "northern forest cassowary". In scientific and biological contexts, "cassowary" continues to refer to the full biological genus *Casuarius*, and the speaking lineage must be specified more precisely. Collapsing these registers either strips the speaking lineage of their self-name or conflates them with their non-speaking relatives in ways that erase biologically and ethically real differences.

The naming distinction is designed to remain uncomfortable. The speaking lineage and their forest relatives are close enough kin that the distinction cannot be clean or comfortable. A speaking cassowary and a northern forest cassowary share anatomy, forest range, frugivory, male parental care, and millions of years of common ancestry. What they do not share is the slow accumulation of manipulation, language, fire use, and institutional life that the speaking lineage built from the Claw Development Era forward. The naming system names that difference — personhood, language, divergence, history — without pretending it makes the two lineages unrelated, and without pretending the closeness is not real.

## Core Reality

- The speaking lineage descends from a southern cassowary population that began diverging from wild relatives during the Claw Development Era under predator pressure. The divergence accumulated across millions of years through manipulation, language, extended juvenile development, fire use, and institutional life — not through a single event.
- Non-speaking cassowary species — northern cassowaries (*Casuarius unappendiculatus* or equivalent), dwarf cassowaries (*C. bennetti* or equivalent), and non-divergent southern cassowary populations — share close common ancestry with the speaking lineage. They are not separate families.
- The speaking lineage's biological ancestry is southern cassowary. Their divergence is real but does not sever that ancestry.
- "Cassowary" as a biological term covers all these species; this creates the naming ambiguity.
- Ordinary language narrows broad category terms into specific people-names through use, not declaration. The speaking lineage does this to "cassowary" the same way any group name narrows over time.
- The narrowing is cultural and linguistic, not a denial of shared ancestry or a claim of biological separateness.

## Naming Convention

**For the speaking people:**

- **"cassowary" / "cassowaries"** — default and correct term for the speaking lineage in social, historical, and narrative contexts. No qualifier needed unless ambiguity requires it.
- **"speaking cassowaries"** — use when clarity is needed that the speaking lineage is meant, particularly in comparative or scientific contexts where the biological genus is also present.
- **"southern-cassowary-derived speaking lineage"** — for technical or evolutionary discussions requiring precision about ancestry and divergence.

**For non-speaking relatives:**

- **"forest cassowaries"** — broad common category for non-speaking cassowary relatives. Ecological and kinship framing rather than scientific. Implies closeness without claiming sameness.
- **"dwarf forest cassowary"** — the non-speaking dwarf cassowary, highland and smaller-forest habitats. Species-level clarity when needed.
- **"northern forest cassowary"** — the non-speaking northern cassowary, lowland and northern range habitats.
- **"southern forest cassowary"** — non-speaking southern cassowary populations that did not inherit the speaking lineage's divergent pathway. Use where it is necessary to distinguish these from the speaking lineage within the southern cassowary species.
- **"wild southern cassowary"** — heavier-handed alternative for non-divergent southern cassowaries. Use only where the distinction from the speaking lineage is specifically the point; avoid as a default.
- **"old forest kin"** — informal, affectionate, or ritual register. Acknowledges close kinship without claiming sameness. Appropriate in temple contexts, kinship speeches, and traditions that emphasise shared ancestry.

## Kinship Logic

Forest cassowaries are close kin, not unrelated animals and not failed or incomplete ancestors. The naming system should preserve this proximity rather than collapse it.

The speaking lineage's divergence was an accumulation, not a separation. Forest cassowaries share the same frugivory, male parental care, female territorial dominance, and forest ecology that the speaking lineage inherited. What they do not share is the specific cascade that began with the wing-claw divergence: the compounding of manipulation, fire use, extended juvenile development, cockatoo guidance partnership, and derived action-language that eventually made institutional life possible. That cascade happened in one lineage under specific pressures. It did not happen universally across all cassowary populations.

A speaking cassowary looking at a northern forest cassowary is not looking at a primitive version of itself. It is looking at a relative that did not follow one particular pressure cascade over millions of years — a relative whose life follows constraints recognisably close to those that shaped the speaking lineage's own biology. The distance is real. The closeness is also real.

The naming system should make both visible. "Old forest kin" preserves the closeness. "Forest cassowary" names the category difference. Neither implies the non-speaking relatives are failures, props, or objects. The distinction is about personhood, language, divergence, and history — not about worth, destiny, or moral hierarchy.

Border communities, highland regions with dwarf forest cassowary populations, and lowland communities near northern forest cassowary range may hold more nuanced kinship attitudes than distant urban populations for whom forest cassowaries are abstractions. Those regional differences belong in later lore.

## Constraints

- "Cassowary" must not be replaced as the project-facing name for the speaking people.
- The speaking lineage is biologically descended from southern cassowary ancestors; they are not unrelated to other cassowaries, and the naming system must not imply they are.
- Non-speaking cassowaries must not become a servant class, a fantasy race distinction, a symbolic prop for the speaking lineage's development, or an occasion for moral hierarchy.
- Naming must not encode evolutionary destiny or imply that the speaking lineage represents the intended direction of cassowary evolution.
- Scientific and biological classification continues to use real-world taxonomic terms. The lore naming convention operates in a different register and does not override taxonomy.
- Common speech terms and technical terms must be kept distinct. Context determines which register applies; a temple speech and a scientific document use different terms for the same relationships.
- The naming system must not smooth away the ethical discomfort of the speaking lineage's proximity to their non-speaking relatives. The discomfort is load-bearing.

## System Implications

- Future lore documents can use "cassowary" by default for the speaking people without disambiguation. Disambiguation is only needed when non-speaking relatives are also present in the same document.
- Reference documents should continue to use real-world biological terms and should specify "speaking cassowaries" or "speaking-lineage cassowaries" when they mean the divergent population rather than the biological genus.
- Lore documents involving territorial disputes, forest ecology, wildlife, or kinship ethics should specify "forest cassowaries" or a more specific term when referring to non-speaking relatives.
- The naming ambiguity creates usable friction for: debates about forest cassowary protection, ritual acknowledgment of shared ancestry, scientific classification disputes, and political rhetoric about cassowary identity and belonging.
- Speeches and philosophical texts can exploit the ambiguity productively. A "Third Cassowary" style address — implying that the speaking lineage and forest cassowaries may not be as categorically separate as cultural convention suggests — becomes available as a rhetorical move without requiring a full mechanism document. The naming conventions established here make that ambiguity structurally available.
- Institutional documents dealing with cassowary territory, law, tribute, or resource access must specify which cassowaries are subject to the institution. The naming system makes that distinction possible.

## Known Variability

- Regional terms for non-speaking relatives vary. Border communities with living forest cassowary populations nearby may use more kinship-sensitive or more ecologically specific terms than inland or urban communities for whom forest cassowaries are distant.
- Scientific, temple, trade, and common speech communities may use different registers for the same relationships. A formal classification document, a kinship funeral speech, and a trade inventory describe the same biological realities through different language.
- Some communities resist narrowing "cassowary" to the speaking lineage only. In areas where forest cassowaries share territory with speaking cassowaries — or historically shared territory — the ambiguity may be culturally preserved and the narrowing resisted as a political or ethical position.
- The speaking lineage's self-name in their own action-language predates any of these English-facing terms and is not specified here. That term may carry its own distinctions and kinship gradations not captured by these conventions.
- The terms "old forest kin" and similar affectionate registers are not universal. Communities with histories of conflict, ecological competition, or predation risk near non-speaking cassowary populations may use colder or more distanced terms.
