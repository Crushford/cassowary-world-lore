# Fire Incubation and Extended Juvenile Development

## Summary

This document explains how domestic fire, egg incubation, cooked food, and longer juvenile dependency interact in Cassowary World. It also explains why cassowary societies can later domesticate other birds through egg capture and chick rearing.

## Atomic Notes

- Cassowary domestic life includes controlled fire in or near homes.
- Fire allows cassowary households to create stable warm spaces for egg incubation.
- Fire-assisted incubation supports longer and more controllable egg development than exposed wild nesting alone.
- Cooked and processed foods help support chicks that remain dependent for longer periods.
- Longer juvenile dependency allows more time for learning, social transmission, and technical skill acquisition.
- Fire incubation creates a transferable technique for hatching eggs from other bird species.
- Because bird chicks do not require milk, stolen or collected eggs from other birds can potentially be incubated and raised by cassowary households.
- This pathway supports later moa domestication in New Zealand and rare dromornithid prestige handling in Sahul.

## Context

Fire incubation is a biological and domestic bridge between cassowary reproduction, household life, and later animal domestication. It gives cassowary societies an egg-based domestication pathway that differs from mammal domestication.

The important step is not simply that cassowaries can keep animals. It is that they already know how to manage warmth, eggs, chick emergence, and early feeding inside domestic spaces. Once this household practice exists, eggs from other bird species can become candidates for managed hatching.

This is especially important for moas. A mammal newborn taken from its mother would require milk or specialized feeding. A moa chick does not. If a cassowary household can incubate a stolen or collected moa egg, the chick can emerge among cassowaries, imprint on handlers, and enter a managed rearing pathway.

The same logic can apply more weakly to dromornithids in Sahul. Dromornithid egg capture and chick rearing may support prestige handling, but their body plan, danger, and ecological role make routine domestication or riding less certain than with moas.

## Limits

Fire incubation does not automatically domesticate other birds. It only makes egg-based capture and imprinting plausible. Long-term domestication still requires surviving chicks, repeatable feeding, controlled breeding, temperament selection, and compatible settlement ecology.

Fire incubation should not be treated as a complete explanation for cassowary intelligence by itself. It is one enabling system among anatomy, predator pressure, food processing, social learning, and extended juvenile dependency.
