# Managed Ant Lineages

## Summary

This document defines ant lineages selected for different environments and management roles. Cassowary societies do not domesticate one universal honeypot ant. They manage and select regional ant lineages for chamber tolerance, replete storage capacity, humidity tolerance, recovery after harvest, and compatibility with local orchard systems.

## Atomic Notes

- There is no single domesticated honeypot ant. The managed population is a regional set of lineages, each selected for different conditions.
- Selection is primarily passive: cassowaries prefer, protect, and harvest from colonies that survive, recover well, and expand into provided chambers. Over generations this constitutes selection without requiring deliberate breeding programs.
- Deliberate selection for specific traits — larger repletes, better chamber acceptance, faster recovery — may develop later, particularly in elite production contexts.
- Lineage terminology is preferred over "breed" or "domesticated species." These are ecological lineages shaped by management pressure, not species-level domestication events.
- Chamber tolerance is the most universally selected trait: colonies that accept and use ceramic chambers are accessible; colonies that do not are left to wild management or abandoned.
- Recovery after harvest is critical. Colonies that rebuild replete capacity within one season are renewably harvestable; colonies that take years to recover are not useful for regular tribute production.
- Humidity tolerance is the key differentiator between dryland and wet-margin lineages. Moving a dryland lineage into a wet-margin system does not work without selection for humid conditions.
- Elite large-replete lineages are a distinct phenomenon: high prestige, high yield potential, high fragility, and likely controlled by specific institutions or groves rather than distributed across village orchards.

## Context

Managed ant lineages are the biological complement to pottery-assisted nest architecture. Pottery changes the physical environment; lineage selection changes the colony's response to that environment. Both are necessary for the system to work reliably.

The reference baseline in [Honeypot Ants](../../reference/species/honeypot-ants.md) describes naturally occurring lineage diversity. Cassowary management works with and shapes that existing diversity rather than creating new species.

## Functional Ant Lineage Classes

### Deep Dry-Store Ants

The closest to real-world natural baseline honeypot ant behaviour.

**Characteristics:**
- Strongest natural replete behaviour. Colonies produce large replete cohorts in good seasons.
- Deep nest architecture. Chambers are naturally dry, thermally stable, and flood-resistant.
- High per-replete storage capacity in optimal conditions.
- Poor chamber acceptance for surface-placed pottery. Nests are already deep and self-structured; artificial surface chambers may be used only marginally.
- Not well adapted to humidity, poor drainage, or shallow soils.

**Management approach:**
- Primary management tool is harvest access rather than chamber assistance. Cassowaries may dig carefully, use narrow-access tools, or exploit natural chamber openings.
- Pottery may assist by providing entrance barriers or expanded access corridors rather than new storage volume.
- Protection from predators and competitors is more important than nest restructuring.

**Ecological role:** Arid and semi-arid zones. Foundation of early discovery and prestige sugar. Not the main engine of tributary surplus.

---

### Chamber-Friendly Orchard Ants

The most important lineage class for managed production.

**Characteristics:**
- Accept and use ceramic chambers placed near nest entrances or expansion zones.
- Store repletes in predictable, accessible locations within chamber systems.
- Recover well after partial harvest — replete populations rebuild within a season in productive conditions.
- Compatible with savanna woodland orchard environments.
- Moderate natural replete capacity, significantly enhanced by chamber assistance.

**Management approach:**
- Pottery-assisted nest localisation is central. Ceramic chambers direct colony expansion into accessible, harvestable space.
- Harvest removes accessible repletes without destroying the core nest structure.
- Colony survival and recovery are built into the harvest rule: never strip a colony completely.
- Orchard management ensures food flow (honeydew) remains stable to support replete rebuilding.

**Ecological role:** Primary production lineage for savanna woodland and village orchard systems. Backbone of cluster-level yield and tribute-district production.

---

### Humidity-Tolerant Ceramic Ants

The wet-zone solution.

**Characteristics:**
- Functional in higher-humidity environments with careful management.
- Accept raised, drained ceramic chambers designed for wet-margin conditions.
- Less naturally storage-heavy than dryland lineages; replete numbers and fill levels may be lower.
- Dependent on cassowary technical management — drainage, chamber maintenance, canopy buffering — to remain productive.
- Vulnerable to fungal nest disease and wet-soil instability without intervention.

**Management approach:**
- Raised chamber design is essential. Chambers must be elevated above waterlogging risk.
- Drainage and humidity management are ongoing.
- Colony health monitoring matters more here than in dry systems because failure modes are faster.
- Orchard hygiene — canopy management, air circulation, parasite control — supports colony stability.

**Ecological role:** PNG highlands margins, wet upland systems, rainforest-edge orchards. Technically prestigious because of the management investment required. Not naturally easy.

---

### Small Reserve Household Ants

Low-yield but culturally and nutritionally important.

**Characteristics:**
- Modest replete storage even in good years.
- Reliable and undemanding compared to production lineages.
- Tolerant of disturbance, partial harvests, and variable conditions.
- Compatible with gardens, home edges, small village groves.

**Management approach:**
- Minimal management required. Occasional chamber placement, protection from opportunistic predators, light harvest.
- Not expected to contribute to tribute; function is household reserve and cultural continuity.
- May be maintained by households without specialist orchard knowledge.

**Ecological role:** Distributed across settled landscapes at household or neighbourhood scale. Important for nutritional resilience and cultural significance, not economic surplus.

---

### Elite Large-Replete Lineages

Prestige production, high fragility.

**Characteristics:**
- Selected over generations for unusually large or numerous repletes.
- Higher per-colony yield potential than standard chamber-friendly orchard ants.
- High fragility: less tolerant of disturbance, more sensitive to orchard stress, harder to recover after harvest damage.
- Labour-intensive management requirements.
- Likely controlled by specific elite groves, institutions, or social lineages rather than distributed widely.

**Management approach:**
- Intensive management: optimal chamber conditions, closely controlled harvest intensity, dedicated orchard attention.
- May be incompatible with shared or common orchard management; require specialist custodians.
- Their high value makes them politically significant — control of elite lineages may confer institutional prestige or economic power.

**Ecological role:** Prestige production in core tribute zones. Not the foundation of generalised surplus. Associated with high-value tribute categories and elite distribution networks. Not assumed to be scalable everywhere.

## Open Questions

- How many generations of management pressure are needed before chamber-friendly behaviour becomes reliably heritable in an orchard lineage?
- Do elite large-replete lineages emerge through deliberate selective practices or through long-term management pressure on surviving colony founders?
- Which specific real-world ant genera are the best biological candidates for each functional lineage class?
- How mobile are managed lineages across ecotypes? Can chamber-friendly orchard ants be moved from savanna woodland to wet-margin environments without long re-adaptation?
