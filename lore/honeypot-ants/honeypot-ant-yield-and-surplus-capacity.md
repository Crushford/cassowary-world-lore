# Honeypot Ant Yield and Surplus Capacity

## Summary

This document answers yield without false precision. Honeypot ant civilisation works through aggregation, not through high individual colony output. Individual colonies produce modest and variable reserves; civilisational surplus emerges from many managed colonies embedded in stable orchard-ant landscapes.

## Atomic Notes

- A single colony rarely feeds a city, settlement, or even a large household reliably. The colony is not the unit of civilisation.
- Many colonies across managed orchards create reliable surplus through aggregation and scheduled partial harvest.
- Yield should be measured at the orchard-ant cluster level, not the isolated nest level.
- Sustainable harvest removes only part of a colony's reserve capacity, leaving enough for seasonal recovery and famine buffering.
- Overharvest causes colony decline, lower future yield, local ecological collapse, and political instability in tribute-dependent regions.
- Yield is qualitative and ecotype-dependent. Pretending to exact kilogram figures is not supported by the system model and should be avoided unless explicitly marked as provisional.
- The principle is: low yield per colony, high reliability per managed landscape.
- Tribute yield is only meaningful across many colonies and many orchards. It is a district-level phenomenon, not a colony-level one.
- Preserved surplus from many colonies becomes politically controllable when sealed into countable vessels. The transition from living replete to sealed jar is where yield becomes administrative.
- Loss of orchard stability, tree health, or sap-feeder populations reduces yield before colony health declines — the system signals stress before collapse.

## Context

This document addresses the frequent assumption that a colony-level description is sufficient to explain civilisational sugar surplus. It is not. The correct unit of analysis shifts depending on the question: ecotype for environmental constraints, colony for biological behaviour, cluster for village-level production, district for tribute accounting.

The real-world baseline in [Honeypot Ants](../../reference/species/honeypot-ants.md) establishes that natural replete storage varies with season, food availability, and colony age. Cassowary World management increases reliability but does not erase this variation. The system is seasonal, ecological, and distributed — not industrial.

[Regional Honeypot Ant Ecotypes](regional-honeypot-ant-ecotypes.md) defines how yield character varies by environment. This document defines the general principles that apply across all ecotypes.

## System Levels and Yield Character

| System level | Yield character | Civilisational role |
|---|---|---|
| Wild nest | Irregular, risky, low | Treat, medicine, emergency food |
| Protected nest | More predictable, still small | Household reserve |
| Managed orchard colony | Seasonal partial harvest | Local exchange |
| Orchard-ant cluster | Many colonies under shared care | Village surplus |
| Tribute district | Many clusters plus preservation | Taxation, storage, redistribution |

## Qualitative Yield Bands

These bands are not exact quantities. They describe functional categories for interpreting orchard-ant system output.

**Trace yield:** Occasional reserve available in good seasons. Not reliable. Useful as treat or medicine. Cannot support consistent household consumption or tribute obligation.

**Household yield:** Enough for small household jars or seasonal supplementation. Supports one household's sugar needs in a moderate year. Important nutritionally and culturally but not a surplus foundation.

**Cluster yield:** Enough to produce regular preserved vessels across a village or grove. Requires multiple managed colonies and a productive orchard environment. This is the threshold at which local exchange becomes possible.

**Tribute yield:** Meaningful only across many colonies, many orchards, and multiple clusters under shared management. Measured in vessel batches from districts, not individual nests. Requires preservation, standardisation, and custodial infrastructure.

**Estate yield:** Measured by standard vessel batches from a managed district. Represents accumulated cluster output from a season. Feeds regional storage centres and redistribution networks. Not produced by any single nest or grove.

## The Aggregation Principle

Civilisational surplus from honeypot ants does not require exceptionally productive colonies. It requires:

1. Many colonies across stable orchards
2. Sustained orchard health so colonies do not collapse between harvests
3. Conservative harvest rules that leave colonies enough reserve to recover
4. Preservation and sealing technology that converts living replete storage into durable administrative surplus
5. Custodial and tribute infrastructure that moves preserved surplus from clusters to districts and beyond

None of these require extraordinary biology. All of them require sustained ecological and social management.
