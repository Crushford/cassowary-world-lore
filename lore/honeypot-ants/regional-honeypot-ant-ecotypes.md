# Regional Honeypot Ant Ecotypes

## Summary

This document defines how honeypot ant systems vary across Sahul environments. Honeypot ant surplus is regional and ecotype-specific. Real-world arid honeypot ants provide the baseline, but Cassowary World extends the system into woodland, upland, wet-margin, and seasonal shelf environments through pottery-assisted nest management, tree lineage selection, aphid stabilisation, and conservative harvest rules.

## Atomic Notes

- There is no universal honeypot ant farm. The productive unit varies by region, ecotype, tree lineage, sap-feeder lineage, ant lineage, pottery practice, and harvest rule.
- Natural replete storage is strongest in dry and strongly seasonal environments, where colonies evolved liquid storage as a survival strategy against scarcity.
- Wet-zone production is a managed expansion of an existing biological behaviour, not the natural baseline. PNG and wet-margin systems are possible because pottery changes nest microclimate and cassowary management changes ecological conditions.
- Savanna woodland systems are the most scalable because they balance tree productivity and nest stability without requiring extreme technical intervention.
- Yield depends on tree, sap-feeder, ant, pottery, and harvest-rule compatibility. All components must align; failure in any one cascades into lower output.
- Regional ant lineages are selected for chamber tolerance, replete storage capacity, humidity tolerance, or harvest recovery — not a single universal trait.
- Regional tree lineages are selected for sap flow, aphid and sap-feeder tolerance, graft compatibility, and recovery after feeding pressure.
- Sap-feeder populations are managed for stable flow, not maximum abundance. Overproductive sap-feeder populations damage host trees and reduce long-term yield.
- Overproduction and overharvest can damage host trees, collapse colonies, and cause local political-ecological failure.
- Coastal shelf and exposed-plain systems are temporally contingent on sea level and can become inaccessible or politically unstable as climate shifts.

## Regional Ecotypes

### Dryland Storage Ants

**Environment:** Arid and semi-arid zones across interior and margin Sahul.

Dryland storage ants are the closest to real-world honeypot ant baseline behaviour. Their natural conditions — dry soils, protected deep chambers, sparse but concentrated sap-feeder resources — are exactly the conditions where replete storage evolved as a survival strategy.

**Characteristics:**
- Strong natural replete storage, with colonies developing large replete cohorts in good seasons.
- Deep nests provide thermal stability and flood resistance.
- High per-colony storage in good years.
- Sparse host plants limit landscape-scale output. Good colony storage does not mean high total sugar yield from a district.
- Individual colonies may be culturally significant — sources of early discovery, prestige sugar, and desert-margin trade — without being economically equivalent to managed woodland systems.

**Role in Cassowary World:**
- Important for early discovery and opportunistic use from `~6-4 MYA`.
- Prestige and ceremonial sugar source even in later periods.
- Desert-margin trade item and exchange token.
- Not the foundation of civilisational-scale surplus; landscape productivity is too limited.

**Main risks:** Drought, sparse honeydew flow in bad years, colony loss, overharvest of rare colonies, difficulty replacing colonies in resource-poor zones.

### Savanna Woodland Orchard Ants

**Environment:** Seasonal woodland, river margins, monsoon edges, volcanic foothills, and open orchard ecologies across Sahul and southern PNG margins.

Savanna woodland systems are the best candidate for scalable early surplus. The environment provides moderate to high honeydew flow from productive host trees, seasonal humidity patterns that are manageable without heavy technical intervention, and nests that can remain dry enough with moderate ceramic assistance.

**Characteristics:**
- Moderate to strong honeydew flow when orchards are well managed.
- Tree productivity higher than arid zones; host plants recover reliably between feeding cycles.
- Nests benefit from pottery-assisted chambers but do not require elaborate drainage or humidity control.
- Colony stability across seasonal cycles is possible with conservative harvest rules.
- Best candidate for regular village surplus, tribute production, and regional redistribution.

**Role in Cassowary World:**
- Core productive zone for tribute and redistribution from `~3-2.5 MYA` onward.
- Village-scale orchard-ant clusters drive most institutional surplus.
- Regional trade in standard sealed vessels originates primarily in these zones.
- Provides the ecological baseline for the honeypot ant contribution to early state formation.

**Main risks:** Seasonal failure in drought years, overharvest by tributary pressure, tree stress from excessive sap-feeder loading, aphid or sap-feeder population crashes, political extraction beyond sustainable yield.

### Wet-Margin Ceramic Ants

**Environment:** PNG highlands margins, rainforest edges, wet upland systems, and volcanic wet-margin ecologies.

Wet-margin systems are not the natural default for honeypot ants. They are a managed expansion of storage behaviour into environments where the natural conditions — high humidity, periodic flooding, fungal load, and canopy instability — work against the dry, stable chambers that natural replete storage requires.

This expansion is made possible by pottery, drainage, raised nest chambers, careful orchard siting, and accumulated management knowledge. It should feel technically prestigious and hard-won, not naturally easy.

**Characteristics:**
- High tree and sap-feeder productivity when orchards are well managed. Lush conditions support dense aphid populations on vigorous host trees.
- Potential for higher peak yield than savanna woodland systems in good years.
- Nests require raised or specially built ceramic chambers with drainage features and humidity control.
- Highly labour-intensive. Chamber maintenance, drainage clearing, fungal inspection, and predator management are ongoing requirements.
- System is fragile. Storm damage, flooding, fungal outbreaks, or loss of technical knowledge can collapse a managed zone quickly.
- Colonies in these zones are selected for humidity tolerance and chamber acceptance — traits that may differ from dryland lineages.

**Role in Cassowary World:**
- Technically prestigious production zone associated with institutional investment and expert knowledge.
- Important for regions where dry-zone options are unavailable.
- High peak yield but variable year-to-year reliability.
- Collapse of a wet-margin system is politically visible because of the investment required to build it.

**Main risks:** Flooding, fungal growth in chambers, chamber rot, aphid disease and parasite outbreaks, storm damage, loss of technical specialists, sap-feeder population crashes from disease.

### Upland Refugia Ants

**Environment:** Cooler, more stable upland systems — volcanic plateaus, highland valleys, protected escarpments — across PNG and Australian uplands.

Upland refugia systems are not high-yield zones. They are reliability zones. Cooler temperatures slow colony and tree metabolism, limiting peak productivity but also buffering against the crashes that hit lowland systems in climate stress periods.

**Characteristics:**
- Lower annual productivity than savanna or wet-margin systems.
- Greater inter-annual stability than lowland environments.
- More slowly recovering trees and colonies; harvest intensity must be lower.
- Particularly useful as refugia for maintaining lineages — ant, tree, and sap-feeder — through glacial cycles and climate stress.
- Good for seed and lineage stock preservation, stable local household reserves, and continuity of technical knowledge.

**Role in Cassowary World:**
- Not a major tribute engine.
- Valuable for lineage preservation, local resilience, and continuity through climate stress events.
- Upland groves may be managed more lightly than lowland orchards, preserving long-lived trees and established nest systems across centuries.
- Institutional importance may exceed economic output in periods of lowland climate stress.

**Main risks:** Slow production, limited host trees at altitude, cold stress on sap-feeders, small colony sizes, difficulty expanding without better lowland conditions.

### Coastal Shelf and Seasonal Wetland Ants

**Environment:** Exposed continental shelf, coastal plains, and seasonal wetland zones — primarily accessible during low sea-level periods and seasonally dry windows in the Pleistocene.

These systems are geographically contingent. They exist during glacial maxima when sea level is lower and shelf areas are exposed as productive lowland. As climate shifts and sea level rises, coastal systems are lost or displaced inland.

**Characteristics:**
- Boom-bust production pattern. Seasonal windows allow strong honeydew flows when conditions align.
- Good transport access during productive periods — coastal and river systems support easy movement of tribute vessels.
- Production may be high in good years and collapse entirely in flood years or after saline intrusion.
- Politically unstable because production shifts with flooding, salinity, and climate cycles beyond local management control.
- Settlement continuity is difficult when ecologies shift on century or millennium timescales.

**Role in Cassowary World:**
- Seasonally significant production zones tied to glacial windows.
- Important for early settlement and exchange during low sea-level periods.
- Politically contested because high-yield zones may be temporary and inaccessible after sea-level rise.
- Institutional investment in these zones must account for their contingent character.

**Main risks:** Salt intrusion, flooding, storm damage, unstable settlement patterns, loss of zone to sea-level rise, political instability around access to temporary productive land.

## Ecotype Comparison

| Ecotype | Natural storage strength | Landscape yield | Reliability | Technical burden | Main risk |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Dryland storage | high | low–medium | medium | low–medium | drought and sparse hosts |
| Savanna woodland | medium–high | high | high | medium | overharvest and seasonal failure |
| Wet-margin ceramic | medium | very high if managed | low–medium | high | flooding, fungus, nest rot |
| Upland refugia | low–medium | medium | high | medium | slow production and limited hosts |
| Coastal shelf | variable | variable | low | medium–high | salinity, flooding, climate shifts |
