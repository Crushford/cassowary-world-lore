# Sap-Feeder Lineages and Honeydew Flow

## Summary

This document defines aphid and sap-feeder types as managed functional lineages in Cassowary World orchard systems. Cassowary societies manage sap-feeders for stable honeydew flow, not maximum infestation.

## Metadata

- Layer: Cassowary World system
- Primary topic: sap-feeder lineages and honeydew flow management
- Topics: aphids, sap-feeders, honeydew, orchard management, ecological domestication, lineage selection
- Era: Ecological Management Era
- Time periods: `~3-2.5 MYA`, `~2.5-2 MYA`
- Regions: Sahul orchard ecologies — functional roles vary by region
- Status: Canon Candidate
- Related species: aphids, other sap-feeding insects, orchard trees, honeypot ants, cassowaries
- Related institutions: orchard management, tribute production, lineage knowledge

## Atomic Notes

- Aphid and sap-feeder abundance is not always good. Excessive sap-feeder populations damage or kill host trees, reducing long-term sugar yield more than modest population control costs in the short term.
- Stable flow is the management target, not maximum abundance. A predictable moderate flow across a full productive season is worth more than a high-intensity flow that crashes.
- Sap-feeder lineages make sugar regional, seasonal, and culturally specific. Different lineages produce different honeydew characteristics, seasonal timing, and host-tree compatibility.
- Predators and parasites may be suppressed, tolerated, or redirected depending on regional ecology and management knowledge. No universal suppression strategy is assumed.
- The useful category is not "aphids" in general but specific lineages with specific properties: flow rate, seasonality, host-tree compatibility, humidity tolerance, and disease resistance.
- Sap-feeder management is an intermediate layer between tree management and ant management. It cannot be separated from either.
- Aphids are the canonical example in Cassowary World lore, but other sap-feeding insects — scale insects, treehoppers, mealybugs, and others — may fill equivalent roles in some regions or on some host trees.

## Functional Sap-Feeder Lineages

### High-Flow Sap-Feeders

Lineages that produce heavy honeydew output when ant protection is strong.

**Characteristics:**
- High honeydew production per individual and per colony.
- High resource demand on host trees.
- Strong ant-protection response — they respond well to ant tending but lose much of their protection value without it.
- Risk of rapid host-tree damage if population density is not controlled.

**Uses:**
- Valuable in tribute orchards with strong host trees, sacrifice tree infrastructure, and experienced managers.
- Associated with higher-value preserved sugar products.
- Not appropriate for young orchards, marginal trees, or systems without sacrifice tree buffers.

**Main risk:** Host-tree damage and long-term orchard decline if used without careful density control and sacrifice tree support.

---

### Stable-Flow Sap-Feeders

The mainstay of household and village orchards.

**Characteristics:**
- Moderate, reliable honeydew production across the productive season.
- Lower host-tree damage risk than high-flow lineages.
- More predictable across different tree ages and seasonal conditions.
- Less spectacular per unit but more sustainable.

**Uses:**
- Standard orchard choice for village-level systems and household reserves.
- Compatible with a wider range of host trees and orchard conditions.
- Preferred for systems where long-term stability matters more than peak yield.

**Main risk:** Susceptibility to predator or parasite outbreaks that suppress populations below useful honeydew threshold. Requires baseline ant protection but does not have high-flow lineage's spectacular ant-response.

---

### Seasonal-Pulse Sap-Feeders

Sap-feeders that boom during specific seasonal transitions.

**Characteristics:**
- Peak populations coincide with leaf flushes, wet-dry transitions, or other seasonal triggers.
- Outside pulse periods, populations are low or dormant.
- Highly productive during pulse windows; nearly absent otherwise.

**Uses:**
- Useful for scheduled harvests aligned with seasonal tribute calendars.
- Allow managers to predict high-yield windows and schedule harvest preparation.
- Valuable in systems where tribute collection is seasonal and predictable timing matters politically.

**Main risk:** Unreliable outside pulse periods. If seasonal trigger fails — late rains, early drought — pulse does not occur and scheduled harvest yields little. High inter-year variability.

---

### Wet-Tolerant Sap-Feeders

Lineages that remain productive in PNG and wet-margin systems.

**Characteristics:**
- Functional at higher humidity and rainfall than most sap-feeder lineages.
- Productive in wet-margin ceramic ant systems.
- Vulnerable to fungal disease, parasitoid wasps, and other wet-zone biological pressure.
- Require orchard hygiene — air circulation, drainage, canopy management — to remain productive.

**Uses:**
- Essential for wet-margin and PNG orchard systems. Without wet-tolerant lineages, honeydew flow is insufficient in those environments to justify the technical investment in ceramic ant chambers.
- Associated with technically prestigious production zones.

**Main risk:** Disease, fungal infection, and parasitoid outbreaks can collapse wet-tolerant populations quickly. System resilience is lower than for dryland or savanna lineages.

---

### Tree-Specific Sap-Feeders

Lineages closely tied to particular host tree varieties.

**Characteristics:**
- Strong compatibility with specific host tree lineages.
- Predictable in mature orchards built around compatible trees.
- Poor transfer between incompatible host trees.
- Difficult to move between regions without compatible trees already established.

**Uses:**
- Valuable in prestige groves built around long-established grafted tree lineages.
- Associated with regional sugar identity — specific taste, seasonal timing, and cultural associations.
- Contribute to the distinctiveness of regional products in tribute and trade.

**Main risk:** Hard to replace if host tree lineage is lost. Dependency on specific trees makes the whole system vulnerable to tree disease, orchard disruption, or loss of grafting knowledge.
