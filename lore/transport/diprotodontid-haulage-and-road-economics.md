# Diprotodontid Haulage and Road Economics

## Summary

This document explains why maintained roads become economically valuable once diprotodontid working lineages exist. Earlier transport networks organize movement across corridors and nodes. Diprotodontid haulage changes the return on route maintenance by making heavy loads movable over distance.

The core claim is not that roads appear because cassowaries want roads. Roads become worth sustained labor because large working animals make bulk transport, tribute forwarding, construction movement, and long-distance redistribution cheaper and more reliable.

## Atomic Notes

- Before diprotodontid haulage, transport is limited by cassowary carrying capacity, terrain, seasonal access, mounted bird limits, and river or coastal availability.
- Diprotodontids allow bulk movement where water, forage, terrain, and route support make heavy animals viable.
- Roads reduce the energy cost and injury risk of moving heavy animals with heavy loads.
- Route maintenance becomes profitable when it increases the load moved per animal, per handler, and per season.
- Heavy haulage makes forwarded tribute and larger storage centers more practical.
- Road value depends on animals, loads, water access, rest nodes, and political protection together.
- Diprotodontid haulage does not replace all transport systems. It adds a slow heavy-transport layer.

## Context

[Transport Networks](../infrastructure/transport-networks.md) establishes that cassowary movement systems begin as maintained corridors and nodes rather than abstract roads. Those routes matter before working animals because tribute, storage, seasonal access, and predator risk already require organized movement.

Diprotodontid haulage adds a new economic threshold. A narrow path can serve foot traffic. A maintained road for heavy animals must support load width, footing, drainage, turning space, rest points, water access, and predictable gradients. That extra labor becomes rational only when the animal can move enough material to repay the maintenance cost.

The domestication pathway is defined in [Diprotodontid Domestication and Working Lineages](../domestication/diprotodontid-domestication-and-working-lineages.md). This document focuses on the infrastructure and exchange consequences after working lineages exist.

## Road Economics

Without heavy working animals, long-distance bulk movement is constrained by cassowary anatomy. Cassowaries can carry, drag, push, and coordinate loads within their biological limits, but sealed tribute vessels, timber, stone, clay, water, and construction materials become expensive to move at scale.

Diprotodontids change the calculation. A trained animal can pull or drag loads that many cassowaries would otherwise move slowly, dangerously, or not at all. This makes route maintenance valuable in measurable terms: fewer broken vessels, fewer handler injuries, larger seasonal loads, more predictable delivery, and better storage-node supply.

Roads reduce friction. A cleared, drained, compacted, and graded route lets a heavy animal pull more weight with less stress. It also reduces hoof or foot injury, load overturning, panic, and delays at mud, roots, slopes, and narrow turns.

Roads also discipline movement. Once loads follow maintained routes, chokepoints become taxation points, storage nodes become redistribution centers, and route authorities gain power by maintaining or denying access.

## Tribute and Storage Effects

Diprotodontid haulage changes [Tribute Collection Counting and Enforcement](../tribute/tribute-collection-counting-and-enforcement.md) by increasing how much collected material can be forwarded beyond local nodes. Earlier tribute remains seasonal, visible, counted, and often local. Heavy haulage makes regional forwarding more reliable.

It changes [Tribute Storage Custody and Redistribution](../tribute/tribute-storage-custody-and-redistribution.md) because centralized storage becomes more practical when sealed vessels, racks, seal materials, timber, and preserved food can move in bulk. Storage centers can draw from wider hinterlands if routes, animals, handlers, and water stops remain functional.

It also changes the economics of [Food Preservation and Storage Systems](../infrastructure/food-preservation-and-storage-systems.md). Preserved sugar paste and other stored foods are already transportable, but heavy haulage allows larger batch movement and makes long-distance redistribution less dependent on many small carrying parties.

## WTA-Style Trade Systems

WTA-style trade systems require regular movement through known routes, exchange nodes, standardized goods, and institutions that can trust delivery. Diprotodontid haulage supports this by moving bulk goods through predictable corridors.

The system is still not frictionless. Water dependency, animal fatigue, route damage, seasonal flooding, predator risk, disease, and handler scarcity all limit throughput. Trade expands where the route economy can maintain animals and infrastructure together.

## Constraints

- Roads are expensive to build and maintain.
- Heavy animals damage weak routes, wet soils, bridges, and storage approaches.
- Water stops are mandatory and can become political chokepoints.
- Route economics fail where terrain is too steep, wet, dense, arid, or unstable.
- Heavy loads move slowly and require planning across seasons.
- Animal injury, disease, panic, or death can immobilize loads.
- Route maintenance must be continuous because vegetation, rain, trampling, and erosion quickly degrade working corridors.

## System Implications

- Road investment becomes more rational after working diprotodontid lineages exist.
- Route authorities gain power by coordinating maintenance, water access, rest nodes, animal movement, and tribute forwarding.
- Storage centers can expand their catchment areas when heavy haulage is reliable.
- Trade networks become more hierarchical because major roads and water-supported routes outperform minor paths.
- Loss of haulage animals or water access can break a route even if the road surface remains intact.
- Bulk transport makes some political centers more durable by tying them to roads, animals, storage, and redistribution.

## Limits

- Diprotodontid haulage is slow heavy transport, not rapid messaging or cavalry.
- It does not replace mounted giant geese, moas, foot couriers, river routes, coastal routes, or local carrying systems.
- It does not make all roads economically worthwhile.
- It does not make bulk transport possible in every region.
- It does not remove ecological constraints on forage, water, disease, or megafauna pressure.
