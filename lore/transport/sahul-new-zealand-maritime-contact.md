# Sahul-New Zealand Maritime Contact

## Summary

This document covers the later regular contact and trade between Sahul societies and established New Zealand cassowary communities. It is distinct from first arrival. Regular maritime contact develops much later, driven by political demand for moas from already-managed New Zealand lineages.

## Atomic Notes

- Regular Sahul-New Zealand maritime contact begins much later than first arrival.
- The route connects Sahul societies to already-established New Zealand cassowary populations with existing moa-handling traditions.
- Sahul does not arrive in New Zealand to begin moa domestication from scratch. It imports animals and lineages that New Zealand communities have already been managing.
- Maritime contact is difficult, seasonal, risky, and institutionally specialised. It is not routine open-ocean commerce.
- The route operates through accumulated route knowledge, specialised crews, known seasonal weather windows, and political demand from Sahul institutions that want moas.
- This route is not evidence of general Sahul maritime expansion or global reach. It is one specific institutionally supported connection.
- Moa import eventually enables the system described in [Moa Mounted Warfare](moa-mounted-warfare.md).

## Context

This document connects the first-arrival and isolation history in [First New Zealand Arrival](first-new-zealand-arrival.md) to the imperial moa use described in [Moa Mounted Warfare](moa-mounted-warfare.md). The gap between the two is not a mystery to be papered over; it is a feature. Cassowaries in New Zealand had time to develop real moa-management expertise before Sahul institutions had the political reach or technical capacity to sustain a crossing.

The trade is not easy and not frequent. It requires coordinated effort, seasonal timing, specialist knowledge, and institutional support. It exists because the moa system is valuable enough to justify the cost.

## Limits

- Do not treat this route as proof of general Sahul maritime capability or global exploration.
- Do not shorten the development time. The route requires institutional maturity that emerges in the Ecological Management Era or later.
- Do not assume moas arrive in Sahul tame. They arrive managed, but still require integration into Sahul breeding and handling systems.
