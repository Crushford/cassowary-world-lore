# Pottery-Assisted Ant Nest Architecture

## Summary

This document makes the pots and nest system visually and technically concrete. Pottery does not create honeypot ant storage. It changes nest architecture by creating dry, protected, predictable chambers where repletes can be accessed and partially harvested.

## Metadata

- Layer: Cassowary World system
- Primary topic: pottery-assisted ant nest architecture
- Topics: ceramic chambers, nest-assist pots, harvest bowls, tribute jars, nest architecture, living storage, dead storage
- Time periods: `4-3 MYA`, `~3-2.5 MYA`, `~2.5-2 MYA`
- Regions: Sahul — all ecotypes, with varying pot forms by region
- Status: Canon Candidate
- Related species: honeypot ants, cassowaries
- Related technologies: pottery, ceramics, sealants, clay working, firing
- Related institutions: tribute, storage custody, redistribution

## Atomic Notes

- Pottery changes where repletes are located, not whether repletes exist. The storage behaviour is biological; the chamber placement is technological.
- Nest-assist pots are living-system infrastructure. They are part of the colony's nest environment.
- Tribute jars are dead-storage infrastructure. They hold processed sugar removed from the living colony.
- Do not conflate nest-assist pots and tribute jars. They have different shapes, functions, sizes, material requirements, and institutional meanings.
- The transition from living replete to sealed tribute jar is the point where biological storage becomes political and administrative storage.
- Pot form matters functionally. Nest-assist pots need rough exteriors, stable placement, and reliable access; tribute jars need sealed necks, standard volume, and authority marks.
- Living nest infrastructure cannot be moved for collection like tribute jars can. Nest-assist pots are in-place infrastructure.
- Ceramic chambers protect nest space from flooding, fungal growth, predators, collapse, and drying — making honey storage accessible in environments where natural nest stability is lower.
- The complexity of pot forms scales with ecotype difficulty: dryland systems need less elaborate chambers than wet-margin systems.

## Vessel Classes

### Nest-Assist Pots

These are not transport jars.

**Visual appearance:**
- Small to medium fired clay chambers — broadly bulbous, squat, or half-ovoid in form.
- Rough outer surface, intentionally textured so that soil, root matter, and organic debris can bind around and against the exterior, integrating the pot into the nest mound environment.
- Narrow throat or side aperture, sized to allow ant traffic but not so wide that humidity control is lost or predator access becomes easy.
- Interior kept clean and stable — ants reject chambers that are too damp, too rough inside, or compromised by fungal growth.
- Removable plug, fitted plug-stone, flat clay cap, or leaf-and-clay lid that allows inspection without destroying nest integrity.
- Sometimes arranged in clusters of two to four around a primary nest entrance, expansion zone, or tree-root junction.
- Partly buried or partly exposed — the buried portion stabilises temperature and moisture; the exposed portion allows access and inspection.
- May show accumulated mineral staining, soil adhesion, and root growth over years of in-place use. Old nest-assist pots develop a patina of integration.

**Size categories:**
- *Hand chamber:* Small enough for one cassowary to lift and position near a nest entrance. The most common and portable form.
- *Root-side chamber:* Larger, designed to sit in a specific position alongside a root junction or orchard mound. Moved rarely if ever once placed.
- *Orchard mound chamber:* Fixed infrastructure, built into or against a managed nest mound. Serviced in place; effectively a permanent architectural feature of a mature orchard site.

**Function:**
- Encourages colony expansion into accessible, predictable storage space rather than deep inaccessible chambers.
- Makes repletes easier to locate without destructive excavation.
- Protects chamber space from flooding, collapse, predator disturbance, and excessive drying.
- In wet-margin systems, chambers with drainage features or raised bases redirect moisture away from replete-storage zones.
- A colony that uses nest-assist pots reliably has, in effect, moved part of its storage into a managed, harvestable space.

**Not:**
- Not a tribute vessel.
- Not removed from the site as tribute.
- Not sealed for long-distance transport.
- Not standardised for counting in tribute accounting.

### Harvest Bowls and Replete Cups

Used during harvest; not long-term storage.

**Visual appearance:**
- Smaller removable ceramic cups, shallow trays, or small detachable chamber inserts.
- Shallow enough for inspection and hand access.
- Practical in appearance — not ceremonial. Often stained with dried sugar residue, soil, and ant activity marks.
- May have residues of resin or clay where they were temporarily plugged or sealed during transfer.
- Simple form: no neck, no elaborate decoration, prioritising ease of use in the field.

**Function:**
- Used by harvesters in orchards during controlled partial harvest.
- Receives repletes or liquid reserve transferred from nest-assist chambers during harvest.
- Allows sorting and separation of harvest material before processing.
- Reduces colony damage: controlled removal into a harvest bowl avoids the destruction that comes from digging into the nest chamber directly.
- Not designed for long-distance transport or long-term storage.

**Not:**
- Not sealed for storage.
- Not a tribute unit.
- Not necessarily standardised — household harvest bowls may vary widely in form.

### Household Preserve Jars

Small to medium sealed storage for domestic use and local exchange.

**Visual appearance:**
- Small to medium ceramic jars with a stable wide base, narrowing to a medium-width neck.
- Sealed with clay, plant resin, or wax-like material after filling.
- May carry simple household or grove marks — incised symbols, impressed patterns, or applied clay tokens.
- Less standardised than tribute jars; form varies by maker and region.
- May hold sugar paste, fruit-sugar jam, or concentrated honeydew mixed with fruit pulp.

**Function:**
- Domestic storage for sugar reserves across seasons.
- Short to medium distance exchange within household networks, village groups, or grove cooperatives.
- Not part of formal tribute accounting unless a household is obligated to contribute to a district.
- May be produced in standard forms within a grove or cooperative even when no external tribute obligation exists.

**Not:**
- Not necessarily standardised for tribute count.
- Not designed for long-distance institutional transport.
- May not carry authority seals.

---

### Tribute Jars

Standardised vessels for counted, sealed, transportable surplus.

**Visual appearance:**
- Standardised form — the form itself conveys institutional intent. A cassowary familiar with the tribute system recognises a tribute jar by its proportions and construction.
- Thick-walled, stable base, narrow neck. Built to survive transport without breakage and maintain seal integrity over weeks or months.
- Fill convention — a standard fill level, indicated by a clay ring, scored line, or standard neck level, so that quantity can be assessed without opening the jar.
- Sealed with clay plug, resin, fibre wrapping, or wax-like material applied to the neck and throat. Seal condition indicates custody integrity.
- Authority mark: an impressed seal, stamped symbol, incised lineage mark, or tied fibre tag that identifies the producing grove or district, the collection authority, and possibly the season or harvest round.
- Designed for preserved sugar paste, fruit-sugar concentrate, or mixed preserved tribute — not for raw repletes or living nest material.

**Function:**
- Converts harvested, preserved reserve into countable, movable, auditable surplus.
- Supports tribute collection, institutional storage, redistribution, and long-distance trade.
- Seal integrity is a custody signal. A sealed unbroken jar is an administrative unit; a broken or resealed jar requires explanation.
- Standard form allows counting and comparison across districts without abstract measurement.

**Not:**
- Not nest infrastructure.
- Not produced at the ant colony. The tribute jar is filled after preservation processing, not in the orchard.
- Not the same as a household preserve jar, even if the two look superficially similar.

---

### Estate and Batch Vessels

Large-volume institutional storage; not hand-carried in routine operations.

**Visual appearance:**
- Large jars or sealed container vessels, possibly as tall as a cassowary's hip or larger.
- Too heavy or awkward for routine hand-carrying after filling.
- Stored in regional centres, estate warehouses, or tribute redistribution facilities.
- May carry multiple authority marks indicating production district, collection round, and storage custodian.
- Used for batch preservation — processing large amounts of harvested reserve at once — or for long-duration institutional storage.

**Function:**
- Institutional storage at regional scale.
- Not direct nest infrastructure.
- Not produced or used at the orchard; only at processing or redistribution facilities.

## The Critical Distinction

**Nest-assist pots are living-system infrastructure. Tribute jars are dead-storage and accounting infrastructure.**

This distinction matters for:

- **Visualisation:** Nest-assist pots look old, dirty, root-bound, and embedded in soil. Tribute jars look clean, sealed, marked, and portable.
- **Harvesting:** Nest-assist pots stay in the orchard. Tribute jars leave it.
- **Institutions:** Nest-assist pots are managed by orchard keepers. Tribute jars are managed by tribute collectors and custodians.
- **Economics:** Damage to nest-assist pots is orchard infrastructure damage. Damage to tribute jars is a custody and accounting problem.
- **Timeline:** Nest-assist pots precede tribute jars. You need the living system before you can fill administrative vessels.
